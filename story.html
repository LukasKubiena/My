Abstract
A famous Buddhist mantra states that the only constant is change. Amidst trump, x, y it is valid that we start asking ourselves if there's anything special to this moment. Every generation experiences changes in … This opens the question how to tell subjective experience of change apart from broader societal one. This thesis aims to answer that challenge by looking at the change in two worlds simultaneously. The material world of physics, atoms, biology, ecology and the social world of laws, nations, constructs and values. When the two grow too far apart we enter what historians call a great transformation. These transformations appear to happen in cycles. In order to better understand this cycle this thesis explores how Thomas S. Kuhn's Structure of Scientific Revolutions can be applied to culture and society more broadly. It asks how we move from one dominant social world to another, and what this reveals about the present moment of technological and social disruption. Kuhn's sequence of normal science, anomaly, crisis, and revolution is extended here to culture: stable value systems sustain order until their assumptions can no longer explain lived reality. When contradictions accumulate, a crisis of meaning arises. The passage to a new paradigm begins not through invention alone but through re-orientation of perception and value. Examining historical episodes, the thesis traces how paradigm transitions unfold across domains—how advances in tools, art, and governance interact to redefine what a society holds true. The contemporary analysis observes parallel conditions: heightened psychological tension, ecological and institutional strain, and rapid technological advance. Read through Kuhn's lens, these form the outline of a paradigm that has begun to lose coherence and the early contours of another still taking shape. The study suggests that lasting renewal depends less on innovation itself than on integration—the slow alignment of technology, culture, and presence into a form of life that can sustain clarity and meaning.

Introduction

We live in strange times. On a daily basis we hear news which seem to upend each other in scope and magnitude. Geopolitical structures that seemed set in stone a few decades ago appear to be changing overnight. Across the world tension between nations increases and many people in the western world are faced with uncertainty on more than one front. In a span of a few years we gained access through the internet and social media to the thoughts and concerns of over 5 billion people globally. We moved from bulky desktop machines to carrying the entirety of human knowledge in our pockets and now watch as artificial intelligence performs tasks that seemed impossible five years ago. By every metric, we have more tools, more wealth, and more capacity than any generation before us, yet we feel less capable.

Parents find themselves struggling to guide children through a world with the values and norms they themselves grew up with and they no longer quite fully recognise. Even with all the tools for connection out there, we report feeling increasingly isolated. Global GDP has tripled in three decades, yet reported happiness in Western democracies remains flat or is falling (Easterlin, 2015). Rates of anxiety are rising, particularly among our younger generations (Twenge, 2017). Economic structures that defined adulthood for previous generations—stable employment, the 9-to-5 workday, homeownership as a realistic goal—no longer seem to hold the same promise. This confuses many of us. We look at the gap between what we were taught and what we experience, and assume something must be wrong. If we don't blame ourselves we look outward and blame politics, the news cycle or technology.

Change has always been part of our lives. Every generation has to adapt to new technologies and new social norms. The human body is biologically wired to resist change and to fear uncertainty. When the future becomes ambiguous, our bodies instinctively react as if we are under threat. Because this biological reaction is constant, every generation assumes the normal friction of growing up to be the breakdown of a world order.

However, while our fear of change is constant, the scale of change is not. There are differences between normal cultural drift and an actual historical paradigm coming to a close.

Occasionally, the world undergoes what historians call a "Great Transformation." (Polanyi, 1944). These are moments independent of our individual experience, where the underlying mechanics of society—how we communicate, how we work, and how we organise our lives—are rewritten in a very short time period. While these moments are hard to quantify, they have happened many times before. It happened when we invented the printing press. It happened when we built the steam engine and when we moved from a predominant country-life to our metropolis'.

The Two Worlds

Understanding how a great transformation unfolds is difficult. History is a messy and very dynamic playing field. Change on an individual and global scale is caused by many different things simultaneously, across different time spans. We couldn't immediately tell the printing press apart from x, and often times only retrospective analysis makes these "epoch shifts" as we learn in high school apart. However, a useful framework for telling "normal change" apart from "greater change" is reducing our complex daily experience down to two worlds we all navigate throughout our lives.

The first is the Material World. This is the world of atoms, biology, and physics. It includes the tools we can build (technology), the planet we live on (ecology), and the biological reality of our own bodies. This world is governed by natural laws. A steam engine generates force because of physics, regardless of who operates it. An internet cable transmits data at a certain speed, regardless of the political system of the country it connects. If a harvest fails or a virus spreads, that is a material reality. It is non-negotiable.

The second is the Social World. This is the layer of human coordination and the world that especially today feels most dominant to us. As we moved from living in small tribes where everyone knew each other to collaborating with millions of strangers, we needed new ways to coordinate our actions. We created "intersubjective realities"—things that exist because we all collectively agree to act as if they exist. You cannot touch a "human right" or hold a "market value" in your hand. A dollar bill is simply a piece of paper; its value lies entirely in our collective agreement to treat it as valuable. A border is a line on a map; its power comes from a collective decision to treat it as real. If we agree the paper has value, we can trade with strangers. If we agree the institution has authority, we can resolve disputes without violence.

The social world solves a particular coordination problem: Every human society, regardless of structure, culture, and era, strives to live what could be called "a good life." Our messy co-existence with billions of strangers is united by our universal aim to solve the same basic problems: food, shelter, safety. Once those are secured, people pursue coordination at scale, then status, meaning, connection. What constitutes "good" varies - a good life in a subsistence farming society looks different from a good life in an industrial economy, which looks different from a good life in a digital one. But the drive itself is constant.

We work towards these goals either on a big scale or just in our day to day work, try to solve them, and turn those solutions into habits, and eventually, vote them into laws. For some, it's about taking care of themselves. For others, it's about their family and some find a lot of meaning by making it about other people, about society, about their city, their neighborhood.

The social constructs we build are attempts to solve these problems at scale. Our social world codifies successful approaches into institutions, laws, economic models, life scripts then passes them to new generations through parenting and education. Given the scale of this pursuit its in no way as easy as it sounds. There are good incentives that encourage cooperation and fairness, but also bad ones that reward short-term gains or exploitation. Some people consciously manipulate systems for personal power. Others subconsciously follow incentives that harm others without realising it.

These constructs are always playing catch-up with reality because material conditions change faster than social agreements can update. A law written for one set of conditions keeps operating after those conditions shift. An education system designed for one economy keeps teaching the same curriculum after the economy transforms.

Unlike the Material World, this layer is constructed by humans. It is inherently sticky. Because millions of people rely on these rules to plan their lives—to decide what to study, where to work, when to have children—we cannot change them easily. We tend to hold onto old agreements long after the conditions that created them have shifted, because stability feels safer than uncertainty.

In a stable society, these two worlds align. The map (Social) matches the territory (Material). We build a school (Social) that actually prepares a child for the economy (Material). We follow a career script (Social) that actually provides security (Material). When the map matches the territory, life feels coherent, safe and plannable.

But these two worlds move at different speeds. The Material World is dynamic; technology and ecology can change overnight. We can move from horse-drawn carriages to automobiles, from letters to instant global communication, within decades. The Social World is generally conservative. Our agreements are written into laws, taught to children, built into institutions, and woven into identities. Changing them requires changing what millions of people believe and expect. This is not to say the Social World is frozen. We have seen how quickly the role of women in the workplace has been redefined, or how the definition of marriage transformed across the Western world in less than a generation. However, the structural agreements—the deep economic models, the legal frameworks, and the life paths we teach our children—are much slower to move.

Eventually, a gap opens up. The territory shifts, but we keep using the old map. We keep performing the same habits —going to the office, taking the test, buying the asset—but they stop producing the result they promised. Our institutions continue to optimize for the old world, measuring success by outdated metrics. We end up performing the rituals of the Social World, but they no longer produce the results in the Material World that they promised.

When this gap grows too large, we end up in moments of "Great Transformations," "Paradigm Shifts," or "Epoch Changes." These are specific periods in our human story where transformation occurs not just in one domain, but across economic, technological, and cultural spheres simultaneously. One established worldview ends, but a new one has not yet arrived.

The Stakes of Transition

We deserve to understand these moments beyond just reading about them in history books. All kinds of change - whether small or big - directly affects our lives. When the frameworks we use to make sense of the world stop providing reliable guidance, it distresses us. We often react by grasping for certainty: avoiding the unfamiliar, clinging to rigid routines, or accepting authorities that promise clear answers (Dugas & Robichaud, 2007).

History unfortunately shows us what happens when we fail to recognize we are living through a great transformation. The rigidity of the Imperial Chinese bureaucracy in the face of Western technological change contributed to a dynasty's collapse. In Weimar Germany, a population "unmoored"—no longer confident in the old framework but not yet convinced of an alternative—became vulnerable to authoritarian certainty of the Nazis. More recently, financial regulators failed to see systemic risk building before 2008, triggering the Great Recession—millions of foreclosures, mass unemployment, and political upheavals whose effects continue to shape our politics today (Tooze, 2018).

Whether you're a graduating student today experiencing job market changes that don't match what you were told about career paths or a worker struggling to keep up with a rapidly changing technology landscape - distinguish personal failure from systemic transitions - matters. If we can face the uncertainty we all experience and read in the news about today, and understand the structure of the change we are in, we stand to regain agency.

During past epoch changes, decision-making often fell to those who understood the transition early, or to authoritarian actors who exploited the confusion. It should be our aim for this understanding to be distributed. Over the next few years, consequential decisions will be made—about AI, about work, about culture - decisions that will be shaped by what we teach your children, how we vote, voice concerns publicly, organise locally, or change the way we work.

To be better prepared to make these decisions, we need a framework to understand what and how change is happening today. Whether the news about stirring political tensions hold truth, whether AI will make large portions of the job market obsolete, and whether climate change will be impacting the spaces we live in. No matter your stance on these issues, when they start affecting the way you live in a few years from now, you deserve to be in the loop.

This thesis explores whether a famous historian's work - Thomas Kuhn's Structure of Scientific Revolutions - can help us understand how these transitions unfold. We will look at how it can be applied to culture, how the current social and material world stand to each other, and what happens when the gap between them grows wider. Finally, we will look at how technology and culture can coexist as tools to help us navigate major transitions ahead.

Theoretical Basis

The Biology of Uncertainty

To understand why this specific moment feels so disjointed—why the post-pandemic world feels like a timeline that skipped a beat, or why the acceleration of technology feels physically exhausting—we need to look at how our own brain makes sense of our daily reality.

For a long time, we thought of the brain as a computer that passively processes information. Modern neuroscience however, suggests that the brain is actually a prediction machine. Enclosed in the dark silence of the skull, it has no direct access to the outside world. It has to make educated guesses of whats out there. Our brain thus constantly projects a model of the world onto our senses, trying to predict what will happen next.

A practical example might help illustrate this: Imagine sitting in a windowless house and hearing a tap on the wall. Is it a woodpecker? A burglar? A branch? To solve this, you need generate a "hypothesis" and guess based on what you know. If you live in a high-crime city, you might guess "burglar". If you live on the countryside you might lean more towards "woodpecker". You compare your guess (the Prediction) against the sound (the Evidence).

Evolutionarily, this guessing game was a survival necessity for us. If our ancestors stood in a forest and saw orange stripes behind the bushes, they could not afford to rely purely on their obstructed perception. They had to predict "tiger" and run. If they processed too long before reacting, we wouldn't have ancestors.

This process of guessing and matching is what our brain does every millisecond of our lives. It runs two competing streams of information: Top-Down (The Prediction): What it expects to encounter based on past patterns. Bottom-Up (The Sensory Input): The actual data reported by our senses. The brain's primary goal is to minimize the difference between these two streams—what scientists call "prediction error." When the prediction matches the input (e.g., you expect a solid floor, you step, and the floor is solid), the brain remains calm and our world feels coherent.

These predictions exist in a Hierarchy of Beliefs. At the bottom are small, fleeting expectations, like "this door will open." At the top are deep, slow-moving convictions, like "I am safe," "my work has value," or "the future is predictable." The Social World shapes these high-level beliefs. It provides the stable platform upon which we stand.

When the Material World shifts—when the economy changes or technology upends the workplace—it violates these deep predictions. The brain has two ways to resolve the resulting pain: Update the Map (Perceptual Inference): We accept the new information. "The floor is rotten; I must be careful." This is learning. We change our minds to better fit the world. Update the World (Active Inference): We act on the environment to force it to match our expectations - "I will fix the floor." If we feel cold, we don't just accept it; we put on a jacket. This changes the world to better fit the mind.

Crucially, the brain often prefers the second option. Updating our deep beliefs is cognitively expensive and destabilizing. So, in the early stages of a paradigm shift, we rarely try to learn. Instead, we try to Active Inference our way out of it. We try to "act away" the error. We double down on old habits. We pass laws to stop technological change. We cling to rigid routines. We try to force the world back into alignment with our old map.

Only when this repeatedly fails—when no amount of effort makes the old rules work—does the brain begin to accept that the map itself is obsolete. This is the Void. It is a quite terrifying interval where we must operate without a reliable model of the world, searching for a new structure that can once again predict our daily experiences better.

To the deep, evolutionary parts of our brain, this uncertainty is indistinguishable from a physical threat. If you cannot predict your environment, you cannot survive. When a society's map stops working, it triggers a collective state of chronic prediction error. We feel it as anxiety, as "brain fog," or as a low-level hum of stress that never goes away. This explains why, during times of deep change, we don't just feel confused; we feel threatened, anxious and under constant pressure. We double down on the old rules not because they are working, but because the alternative—the void of having no rules—is terrifying to our biology.

The role of technology, culture and politics

We have established that our societies can occasionally undergo a great transformation. Now we want to look at the mechanics of these transitions more specifically. We can broadly identify three distinct players that drive the way our world changes: Technology, Politics, and Culture. It is a messy interplay and there is not always a clear distinction, but generally, we can say: Technology often creates the change; Culture expresses and shapes it; and Politics governs it.

Technology, like the steam engine or AI today, often acts as the primary catalyst. It is the force that alters the Material World most directly. Whether it is the steam engine, the birth control pill, or the algorithm, technology changes the "conditions of possibility" before society has agreed on new rules. It operates with indifference to the social order; it simply creates new capabilities and constraints.

Politics attempts to govern these changes. Nonetheless, it is a poor indicator of explaining the shift. Sometimes politics ignores it, sometimes - like in our modern political discourse - it becomes obsessed with the idea that "the system is broken" and that "change needs to happen". However, when politicians encounter the friction of a paradigm shift, they are incentivized to instantly convert that ambiguity into a clear, actionable narrative. They channel the public's anxiety into established categories: "Us vs. Them," "Globalism vs. Nationalism," or "Labor vs. Capital." They acknowledge the symptoms of the shift but interpret them through the lens of the old map. A politician cannot say, "I don't know what is happening." They must say, "This is happening because of [X], and if you vote for me, we will return to stability." Therefore, while politics is loud, it is a low-fidelity sensor. It compresses complex, novel signals into rigid, pre-existing structures.

This is not to say that politics does not play a vital role in paradigm shifts. At its best, politics also codifies the shift. It takes the new reality and eventually writes it into law. The New Deal in the 1930s, for example, was a political response that institutionalized a new understanding of the economy after the old map collapsed. However, the structural function of politics is to govern, and governing requires certainty.

Most crucial for this thesis, is the role of the third player - What we call "culture". "Culture" here refers to the entire sphere of the way we express ourselves — the collection of stories we tell, the habits we live out, and the values we share. From our digital discourse and memes to aesthetics and social behavior. These are the spaces where our "Social World" is negotiated daily. Because the stakes are lower than in politics of finance, cultural spaces allow for agency. They possess Freedom without Consequence.

Technology changes our environment, but culture changes us. When technology creates a new reality—like the ability to work from anywhere or the presence of AI—we don't immediately know how to behave. There is no manual, so we look to the culture around us - we observe how our friends raise their children, how the news talk about it, how our peers are prioritizing their time - we gravitate toward stories and conversations that give a vocabulary to the vague feelings we are experiencing.

At its simplest level, human expression acts as a time capsule. Every painting, building, poem, meme inevitably captures the reality of the moment it was created. It records two things: what was possible to build (the materials and techniques available) and what it felt like to live (the values, anxieties, norms of the time).

In a stable world, expression usually mirrors the established order—painting a king as heroic, a landscape as peaceful. But in a shifting world, expression begins to record the friction. Vienna at the turn of the twentieth century demonstrates this clearly. The Secessionists didn't just make art—they created new forms of expression that matched what it felt like to live through industrialization's transformation of human experience. Freud developed psychoanalysis partly in response to the psychological strain of urban industrial life. The cultural movements arose from a sense that something in the new material conditions was incompatible with human needs, and existing forms of expression weren't adequate to capture that incompatibility.

Expression serves a dual function: it allows the individual to process internal states, but it also communicates those states to the collective. When enough people recognize the same feeling in a piece of art, a meme, a song—when they say "yes, that's what it feels like"—that shared recognition becomes the foundation for new social constructs.

Before a new value can be voted on in parliament or written into a constitution, it must first be felt in a movie theater, argued about at a dinner party, practiced in a living room. Culture is the rehearsal for the future. It's how we agree on who we want to be before we build the laws that codify it.

The biological reaction to change we established earlier - our state of stress, uncertainty and fear, is not only an unfortunate reality but a useful tool to navigate societal transitions. We collectively not only feel the need to "express" it - any state of strong emotions will need to go somewhere - but also to start experimenting in the void to find new ways forward.

Whether European coffeehouses in 1900 or a TikTok feed in 2025 - they are the playgrounds where people express the friction they feel in their daily lives. Designers, writers or artists can say "the system makes no sense", "this feels wrong," and "this feels weird" without needing to vote on a solution immediately. They can present a reality that is simultaneously broken and beautiful, technological and biological. Crucially, in this case we all count as artists. This might feel counterintuitive, but every human form of expressing oneself - multiplies across millions - changes the defining attributes of our "Social world". We will explain this dynamic in more detail in later sections of this paper.

The relationship between these three is a complex, omnidirectional loop where any one of them can serve as the catalyst. Technology catalyzes change by altering capability (what is physically possible). Politics catalyzes change by altering incentives (what is structurally rewarded). Culture catalyzes change by altering desire (what is collectively valued). While the spark can originate in any layer—a political decision to fund a moonshot is just as transformative as the invention of the microchip—the turbulence of our current moment arises from their interplay and speed at which they change. Technology evolves exponentially (the speed of invention); Culture evolves memetically (the speed of communication); and Politics evolves legally (the speed of consensus). But regardless of which force initiates change, culture expresses it.

All in all, this is just to say that in comparison to most scientific accounts of paradigm change, this thesis views cultural output, albeit noisy and complex, as a critical dataset for understanding our moment of change.

The Mechanism of Change: Kuhn's Framework

In a moment we will dive into a historical example of a paradigm change, in order to help us better understand our current moment. We have established that our brains crave certainty, and that culture is the tool we use to process change. But given that these transitions are a messy and complex interplay of many different parts of society, it would be useful to have a framework that helps us find a pattern amongst the chaos.

One domain where the structure of change has been studied extensively is science itself. The philosophy and history of science developed detailed frameworks for understanding how knowledge transforms, precisely because scientific communities needed to make sense of their own revolutions while they were happening. To understand how these two worlds—the Physical and the Social—drift apart and realign, we can therefor borrow from the history of science. Science is, effectively, the disciplined practice of map-making. It is the attempt to create a mental model (Social) that accurately describes natural laws (Physical). Because scientific communities are explicitly dedicated to matching their theories to physical evidence, they offer the clearest possible laboratory for observing what happens when that matching process fails.

In 1962, Thomas S. Kuhn, a physicist turned historian who is nowadays famous for coining the term "paradigm shift," challenged the prevailing view that scientific knowledge accumulates steadily toward truth. In The Structure of Scientific Revolutions, he argued that even in hard sciences, knowledge advances through discontinuous revolutions. He identified a pattern: communities work within a shared framework (a "paradigm") that defines what the world is like, which questions are legitimate, and what counts as satisfactory answers. This works fine until researchers encounter data that conflicts with the paradigm's predictions - anomalies. Initially these are ignored or explained away. But when anomalies accumulate beyond a certain threshold, the paradigm enters crisis. The community splinters. Professional certainty dissolves. The crisis resolves only through revolution - emergence of a new paradigm that reinterprets the data fundamentally differently.

Kuhn called this shift "incommensurable" - the old and new paradigms aren't just different descriptions of the same world, they represent different worlds. Proponents use the same vocabulary to mean different things, making communication across the divide difficult. The transition isn't gradual accumulation of facts but sudden transformation of vision - a "Gestalt switch" where the same data suddenly organizes differently, like a duck-rabbit illusion flipping.

Kuhn wrote specifically about scientific communities - bounded groups with shared training and clear criteria for problems. He warned against casual extension beyond that context. Cultural domains lack unified training, consensus on methods, obvious criteria for when anomalies truly challenge frameworks versus just being normal noise - a familiar problem we mentioned above.

However, scholars have applied Kuhn's framework beyond science since the 1970s. Sociologist George Ritzer argued sociology itself was a "multiple paradigm science" with competing theoretical frameworks (Ritzer, 1975). Physicist Fritjof Capra extended Kuhn's work further, arguing modern civilization was undergoing paradigm shift from mechanistic to holistic worldview, defining "social paradigm" as "a constellation of concepts, values, perceptions, and practices shared by a community, which forms a particular vision of reality" (Capra, 1982, p. 30). The patterns Kuhn described - working within failing frameworks, accumulating things that don't fit, old vocabulary not capturing what's happening, proliferation of competing approaches during crisis - appear in many human systems relying on shared models of reality. It therefore seems plausible for us to take the liberty of borrowing from Kuhn's framework to make sense of our current times of change.

The translation from Kuhn to culture

We can now try to synthesise all the elements we talked about—the structural drift of society, the physiological mechanism of the brain, and the diagnostic function of culture—into a single coherent framework.

The logic of translation is straightforward. If a "paradigm" is a shared model of reality used to solve problems, then a "culture" is a paradigm writ large - shared assumptions about how life works, used by millions to coordinate daily existence. These assumptions work until underlying reality changes enough to break them.

Stage 1: Normal Culture — The Social World accurately maps the Material World. Charles Taylor calls this the "social imaginary" - the shared background understanding that makes sense of our lives, "the ways people imagine their social existence, how they fit together with others" (Taylor, 2004, p. 23). When the map matches territory, we follow scripts - "get a degree, get a job, buy a house" - and predicted outcomes occur. Institutions fit current demographics. The economy fits current technology. Trust is high because rules work. The brain's prediction error is low. The world feels coherent.

Stage 2: Anomalies Accumulate — As the Material World shifts, "anomalies" accumulate. The career path leads to precarity. The medicine manages symptoms but does not cure. The political promise doesn't match the outcome. Perhaps the birth rate drops (demographics), or automation rises (technology), or the climate shifts (ecology). But the Social World holds its shape. We keep the old pension laws despite the new demographics. We keep the old work week despite the new automation. Initially, as Kuhn noted, we try to ignore these anomalies. We treat them as personal failures or temporary glitches.

Stage 3: Legitimation Crisis (The Void) — When anomalies multiply, social agreement loses authority. Habermas described "legitimation crisis" as structural failure where established tools can't solve problems anymore (Habermas, 1975). This creates a "void." The old rules are technically in force—you still have to go to the office, you still have to pay the debt—but everyone knows they no longer make sense. The shared story has dissolved, but a new one has not yet been written. The "Search" is not a coordinated scientific project; it is a chaotic scattershot of coping mechanisms. Because the "Void" feels like a survival threat to the brain, it triggers a rush for safety. This is why periods of experimentation often coincide with the rise of rigid dogmas, conspiracy theories, or authoritarian movements—attempts to force certainty onto an uncertain world. To fill this void, society enters a period of radical searching. This phase often produces both experimentation and rigid dogmatism. Some experiments try retreat (nostalgia). Some try acceleration (techno-optimism). Some try escape (separatism). Most fail. They will prove too expensive, too strange, or too difficult to sustain. But this variance is necessary. Society uses cultural subcultures to test different ways of living to see what actually works.

Stage 4: Revolution (Integration) — The cycle resolves not when a perfect system is found, but when a framework emerges that is sufficiently compatible with the new reality to stabilize the void. This is rarely a single, uniform answer. Often, several successful experiments begin to converge, or competing frameworks battle for dominance (like the clash between state-socialism and liberal-capitalism in the 20th century). However, the defining feature of this phase is that a new coherent logic takes hold that connects the new Material conditions (the technology, the ecology, the biology) with human needs. It works well enough to resolve the chronic friction. It allows people to live, work, and plan again. Viable experiments scale from fringe to center. They stop being "counter-culture" and become simply "culture" - the new normal.

Often times this process is generational. Sociologist Karl Mannheim observed that a "generation" isn't just people born at the same time. It's people who internalized how the world works during the same historical period. A generation is effectively a cohort that internalized the same "Social Imaginary" during their formative years. Someone who entered adulthood in 1970 learned a different set of assumptions about housing, debt, and climate than someone who entered adulthood in 2010, simply because the material conditions were different.

When you come of age, you're learning two things simultaneously. First, you're learning the material conditions - what actually works, what resources are available, what constraints exist. Second, you're learning the social rules that claim to navigate those conditions. If the rules match the conditions during your formative years, they become your baseline reality. You internalize them not as "one possible way things could work" but as "how things work." If you then participate in building or maintaining those social structures - through your work, your votes, your parenting - you develop investment in them. Not just financial investment, but cognitive and emotional investment. These structures worked for you. They represent your accumulated wisdom about how to navigate the world. When someone suggests they're broken, it doesn't just challenge your opinions. It challenges your entire framework for making sense of reality.

Younger generation faces different circumstances. They enter when material conditions have shifted but the social constructs haven't updated yet. They're taught the same rules - "do X, get Y" - but when they try to apply them, the results come out different. They have no cognitive investment in the old constructs because those constructs never worked for them. They don't see anomalies as errors to be fixed. They see anomalies as the baseline. This creates "incommensurability" - The Older Cohort internalized the agreement during the period of Alignment. To them, the old map feels like nature. When they say "hard work leads to a house," they are reporting a fact of their lived experience. They try to "fix" the anomalies to restore the old world. The Younger Cohort enters the system during the Crisis. They view the anomalies not as errors, but as the baseline. When they say "hard work does not lead to a house," they are also reporting a fact of their lived experience. They do not try to repair the old map; they eventually build new agreements that match the physical reality they actually live in. The older generation tries to fix the anomalies - work the system harder, restore the old patterns. They have investment in those patterns. The younger generation looks for new patterns because they have no reason to believe the old ones ever worked. This isn't a moral difference. It's a structural difference in what each group observed during their formative learning period. This explains Max Planck's observation that new truths do not triumph by converting opponents, but because "a new generation grows up that is familiar with it" (Planck, 1949, pp. 33–34). Unlearning a foundational map is cognitively expensive. It is often easier for society to wait for a demographic shift than to force a psychological one. This explains the cyclical nature of social change. Each generation inherits constructs from the previous one. When conditions are stable, the constructs keep working, so they get reinforced and passed forward. When conditions shift faster than constructs can update, the gap appears. The older generation, invested in constructs that worked for them, tries to maintain them. The younger generation, experiencing constructs that don't work, eventually builds new ones. Like in Kuhn's model, new generations simply do not see the anomalies as errors to be fixed; they view them as the starting point. Their perception has undergone a Gestalt switch. The new generation views the world through a new lens, enabling them to update social constructs to match the new reality. This could be adjusting the education system to integrate AI rather than ban it, or redesigning healthcare to acknowledge the biological reality of mental health rather than treating it as a character flaw.

A tale of Modernism

We have now established a theoretical framework: societies function when their Social World (their agreements, laws, and norms) aligns with their Material World (their technology, economy, and environment). When these two worlds drift apart, the result is friction, followed by a chaotic search for new rules. To observe this mechanism in action, we need a good historical environment. We need a specific time and place where the Material World changed radically while the Social World remained rigidly fixed. We all know stories of change, we learn about them - The Renaissance, the Enlightenment, the Industrial Revolution. No single story covers the entire Western experience. It is very difficult to pick one for this thesis. Our modern world was shaped by many transformations, and telling our entire story would go beyond the scope available to me in this thesis.

Central Europe between 1890 and 1938 serves as this laboratory. The "Material World" had changed completely—rapid industrialization, mass migration, the birth of the metropolis—but the "Social World" (the class hierarchies, the aesthetic values, the imperial structures) refused to move. While this analysis focuses on a special European city - Vienna - the friction was pan-European. By 1900, the entire continent was grappling with the same structural rupture: the collision of 19th-century institutions with 20th-century realities. From the smokestacks of Manchester to the avant-garde salons of Paris, the industrial "Material World" was everywhere eroding the old "Social World." However, in Vienna, the system was so brittle, and the pressure so high, that the cracks became visible earlier, and deeper, than anywhere else.

In the annals of European history, cities like Vienna around 1900 occupy a singular, almost mythological position. In Vienna's case it is frequently portrayed as a golden age of aesthetic refinement—a city of waltzes, whipped cream, and the glittering facade of the Ringstrasse—followed by the tragic collapse of the Habsburg Empire. However, beneath this mythology Vienna between 1880 and 1920 provides a documented case of a society undergoing "a great transformation" within a few generations. A generation grew up assuming permanence, watched their world transform faster than institutions could adapt, and left extensive records of what that felt like. It allows us to observe a well documented case of a society where the "social imaginary" held absolute dominance, the specific anomalies that undermined it, and the frantic attempts by a culture to articulate a new reality before the old one disintegrated. Unlike London or Paris, where democratic institutions acted as pressure valves, Vienna was the capital of a rigid, dynastic empire that tried to hold the old world together by sheer force of will. Our view into this laboratory is partial. Much of the surviving documentation—diaries, psychoanalytic theory, high art—comes from the bourgeois and elite sectors. The working class, the Bettgeher, and the rural migrants who bore the heaviest physical weight of the transition were largely excluded from the means of recording it. Their experience survives in mortality statistics rather than in manifestos. But within these constraints offers a view of multiple systems—political, economic, cultural—breaking down simultaneously within a single city limits.

3.1 Normal Culture: The Habsburg Empire - A World of Security (1860s-1880s)

Vienna was the capital of a dual monarch empire stretching from the Alps to the Russian steppes, governing a population of over 50 million people and ruling over vast territories now comprising twelve modern nations. It was a melting pot of languages and religions—and a robust Kuhnian paradigm - held together by the singular authority of the Emperor. For most people alive in 1880, he was the only emperor they'd ever known. Stefan Zweig, a famous Austrian writer and memoirist, writing about this period, described the mindset of the middle class as the "Golden Age of Security." He remembered his father's generation as men who never ran up stairs, who smoked the same cheap government cheroots as Emperor Franz Joseph, who assumed the world they knew would continue indefinitely. The defining characteristic of this "Normal Culture" was clear: linear progress was inevitable, science had tamed the irrational, and if one followed the rules of the middle class—education, thrift, propriety—the outcome felt safe and realistic.

The system excluded most people from power—women had no vote and limited legal autonomy under civil law that classified them alongside minors (Anderson, 2014). Jews faced persistent discrimination despite legal emancipation (Rozenblit, 1984). Factory workers labored long hours under dangerous conditions for low wages (John, 2009). Ethnic tensions simmered—Czechs resented German dominance, Hungarians pushed for autonomy, Italians looked toward unification movements (Judson, 2016).

3.2 The Accumulation of Anomalies

Demographic changes. Industrialization accelerated in the 1880s and 1890s, transforming Vienna from a center of handicraft production into a major industrial city (John, 2009). Vienna's population nearly doubled between 1880 and 1910—from roughly 700,000 to 2 million. Rural provinces filled tenements in rapidly expanding districts (Judson, 2016). Infrastructure couldn't keep pace with this growth. Housing was severely overcrowded, sanitation was inadequate, and public health crises were frequent (John, 2009). A health inspector visiting the outer districts in 1910 documented five connected rooms containing 38 beds used by more than double that number of people. Ventilation was impossible, creating what the inspector called "a dreadful atmosphere". (Wiener Bezirkskrankenkassa, 1910, as cited in Montags-Post, November 13, 1911). A new category of tenant emerged: the Bettgeher ("bed-goer"). These were workers who rented not a room, but a shift in a bed. One worker slept during the day, another at night. By 1910, approximately 80,000 people in Vienna lived this way (Blau, 1999). The economic base supporting the old social arrangements was changing faster than the arrangements themselves could adapt, all while the people in power - Emperor Franz Josef I - found much of modern life to be "incomprehensible to him."

Political crisis. This friction extended to the political system. By the late 1890s, as the demographic makeup of the city shifted, political structures started failing to manage these tensions. The Austrian parliament—the Reichsrat—descended into chaos as ethnic factions obstructed each other (Boyer, 1981). Czech representatives demanded language rights and equal recognition, while Pan-German nationalists blocked legislation in response. Unable to debate, Parliamentary sessions regularly ended in physical fights and furniture throwing (Judson, 1916). Mark Twain attended the Austrian Reichsrat on October 28, 1897, and observing these events from the gallery, captured the sheer insanity of the spectacle: "Then burst out such another wild and frantic and deafening clamor as has not been heard on this planet since the last time the Comanches surprised a white settlement at midnight. Yells from the Left, counter-yells from the Right, explosions of yells from all sides at once, and all the air sawed and pawed and clawed and cloven by a writhing confusion of gesturing arms and hands." Representatives brought hunting whistles and sleigh bells into the chamber, screaming and slamming desk lids for hours to drown out speakers. The police eventually dragged elected officials out by force (Schorske, 1980). During one brawl, Georg Schönerer "lifted one of the heavy ministerial fauteuils—some say with one hand—and threatened members of the Majority with it". That session lasted over 33 hours and accomplished nothing. Dr. Otto Lecher gave a 12-hour filibuster speech—the longest uninterrupted speech in parliamentary history to that date. Between 1897 and 1904, the legislative branch effectively ceased to function. The Prime Minister was forced to rule by emergency decree (Boyer, 1981). The "unrest in the Imperial Council soon spilled over into the streets." This was a terrifying realization for the "civilized" Viennese bourgeoisie: the thin veneer of order maintained by the police and the emperor could be stripped away in an afternoon. The crisis was amplified by the Viennese press. Viennese paper's adopted a "sensationalist" and "nationalistic" tone, whipping up the fervor of the German population. Observing this descent into chaos was the satirist and critic Karl Kraus. For Kraus the corruption of the word in the feuilleton (the opinion sections of the newspapers) had corrupted the capacity for thought. If the press described the world in decorative, lying phrases, the people would eventually act in irrational, violent ways. Kraus's aphorism, "The secret of the demagogue is to make himself as stupid as his audience so they believe they are clever as he".

Psychological crisis. As the external structures of the city became increasingly chaotic, the internal world of the Viennese subject began to fracture. The medical profession identified a new epidemic sweeping the city: neurasthenia. Originally termed the "American disease" due to its association with the speed and stress of modern American life, neurasthenia found a particularly virulent host in Vienna. The symptoms of neurasthenia were a catalogue of urban anxieties: "headache," "sleeplessness," "neuralgic pains," and a "widespread feeling of uneasiness and restlessness". While often associated with the "white upper middle-class" and "brain workers," in Vienna the diagnosis crossed class lines, affecting both the aristocracy and the working poor. It was the somatic expression of modernity. The human nervous system, evolved for a slower, agricultural existence, was collapsing under the sensory bombardment of the metropolis. For men, it was often linked to anxieties about "overcivilized effeminacy" and the loss of "masculine" vitality in the sedentary office job. For women, it was linked to the "monotonous household life" and the suppression of intellectual and sexual agency.

Crisis of Youth. The "nervousness" of the city took a particularly tragic turn among the youth. Vienna was in the middle of a "suicide epidemic" among students in the secondary schools (Gymnasiums). Stefan Zweig, in The World of Yesterday, describes the school system as a rigid, soul-crushing machine that demanded absolute conformity. The fear of "bad marks," the pressure to succeed in a shrinking economy, and the "faulty upbringing" that emphasized obedience over emotional development led to "acute states of nervous anxiety". Suicide became a morbid trend, a final act of revolt against the "civilized" expectations of the fathers. The "inferiority complex combined with nervousness" created a lethal cocktail.

Freud's Intervention. It was into this atmosphere of nervous exhaustion and repressed violence that Sigmund Freud launched his psychoanalytic revolution. Freud had published The Interpretation of Dreams in 1899 (dated 1900). Through the 1900s and early 1910s, he was developing psychoanalytic theory and building an international movement. Freud was treating patients whose symptoms—hysteria, neurosis, anxiety—the prevailing medical paradigm couldn't explain through physical causes alone. His turn inward to unconscious drives, childhood experience, and symbolic interpretation offered a different map for understanding mental suffering (Gay, 1988). This generated immediate conflict with the previous generation. Freud was repeatedly denied a professorship despite his qualifications. Sigmund Freud's work can be read as a direct response to the Liberal failure. The Liberal father (the Rational Man) was dead. Freud showed that the human mind was not a parliament of reason, but a dungeon of irrational drives (the Id), kept in check by a repressive censor (the Super-Ego).

3.3 Paradigm Crisis

For younger people entering adulthood in the 1890s the established paradigm started cracking more and more. As material, political, and aesthetic anomalies accumulated, the crisis registered psychologically. Austrian novelist Hermann Broch diagnosed this internal state, coining the term Wertvakuum—value vacuum. For Broch, the crisis wasn't merely political or economic but ethical. The central unifying value system that had organized society-whether religious authority or the cohesive feudal order-had disintegrated, but nothing had replaced it (Broch, 1932/1984). Broch argued that Vienna was a center of this value vacuum. The liberal era had attempted to fill it with cultural decoration-the eclectic styles of the Ringstrasse, theater, operetta. But this was superficial, what Broch called "Kitsch"-a substitute for genuine ethical substance (Broch, 1932/1984, p. 7). The architectural historicism of building parliaments like Greek temples and town halls like Gothic cathedrals was an attempt to mask the vacuum by borrowing legitimacy from the past. It was a museum-like culture, looking backward because it lacked confidence to look forward. For Broch "the old values were still vigorous enough to make the creation of new ones impossible" (Broch, 1932/1984, p. 7).

3.4 Paradigm Change

On June 28, 1914, an assassin shot Archduke Franz Ferdinand in Sarajevo. Zweig: "a shot that in a single second was to shatter the world of security and creative reason in which we had been reared, as if it were a hollow clay pot breaking into a thousand pieces" (Zweig, 1942/2013). Emperor Franz Josef I was 84 in 1914, having ruled for 66 years, viewed the assassination as divine retribution: "For me, it is a relief from a great worry." General Franz Conrad von Hötzendorf proposed Serbian war twenty-five times between January 1 and January 1, 1914 (Sondhaus, 2000). These decision-makers applied 19th-century dynastic thinking to 20th-century industrial warfare and came from a generation who couldn't perceive what industrial warfare meant. The Habsburg Empire dissolved in 1918. Zweig: "All the bridges are broken between today, yesterday and the day before yesterday" (Zweig, 1942/2013). Post-war Austria experienced hyperinflation (1921-22), banking crises (1926-27), the Great Depression (1929), political paralysis (Pauley, 1981).

3.5 A tale of culture

We established that culture - the low-stakes space where people express what they're experiencing - is often where a society registers a crisis first. It is no coincidence that Vienna during this time was not only the center stage for one of the most violent political crisis of the time, but simultaneously became the most vibrant cultural laboratory in Europe.

As the "void" between the map (Social World) and the territory (Material World) widened, specific groups began to organize responses. The official culture—the "Potemkin City" of Vienna —insisted on a version of reality that was historically decorated, logically coherent, and morally sanitized. The buildings on the Ringstrasse were built of brick but covered in plaster to look like stone, a literal architectural lie designed to project a stability that did not exist. The old order said art should beautify and celebrate - show idealized bodies, rational triumph, stable hierarchy. But younger people were experiencing nervousness, chaos, housing crises. The collision was between performing stability and expressing what people actually felt.

The founding of the secession. The old art establishment in Vienna - the Künstlerhaus - controlled who could exhibit and sell work. They wanted art that celebrated the monarchy and maintained the illusion of continuity. This didn't work for younger artists trying to express what they actually saw living in the industrial city. In 1897, nineteen artists led by Gustav Klimt broke away and founded their own exhibition space - the Vienna Secession. They carved a motto above the entrance: "To every age its art, to every art its freedom." This was a direct refutation of the Ringstrasse's historicism, which sought to impose the art of past ages onto the present. The building attracted more curiosity and ridicule than any other constructed in Vienna. One writer claimed the dome made viewers think they were facing "a Buddhist temple, mausoleum or crematorium" (Wien Geschichte Wiki). The Secessionists sought to align the aesthetic environment with the modern sensibility. Their solution was rooted in the idea of the Gesamtkunstwerk (Total Work of Art). They believed that by redesigning the environment—the building, the painting, the spoon—they could harmonize the fragmented modern soul. It was a noble attempt, but as we will see with the Wiener Werkstätte, it ultimately failed to scale.

Klimt's faculty painting scandal. The clash became visible in 1900 when Klimt delivered paintings commissioned for the University. The Ministry expected conventional allegory - Philosophy depicted as wisdom triumphing, Medicine as heroic healing. Klimt painted tangled naked bodies, biological helplessness, existential confusion. He was painting what he saw: that humans are fragile, that the world is chaotic, that rationality doesn't control everything. 87 professors signed a petition demanding the paintings never be installed. The newspapers called them "pornographic." Allgemeine Zeitung (Munich, June 11, 1900): "an entirely incomprehensible Symbolist painting... This bundle of naked figures, interspersed with eerily grimacing faces, makes viewers run for the hills." When Medicine arrived in March 1901, the scandal intensified and politicians raised the matter in Parliament—the first time a cultural debate had ever reached that legislative body. Authorities confiscated the 6th edition of the Secessionist magazine because preparatory sketches threatened "public morality" (Natter & Frodl, 2012). By 1905, Klimt withdrew the paintings, returned the commission money and retreated into a private world of art and patronage - his daily routine becoming rising early, swimming in the lake, rowing to "shake the muscles," and painting in his studio. When officials insisted the paintings were state property, Klimt reportedly threatened removal staff with a shotgun. He painted a new work originally titled To My Critics (later Goldfish) - featuring a nude woman showing her backside to the viewer. Similar movements spread across Europe. Movements like Die Brücke (The Bridge), founded in Dresden in 1905, and the Austrian Expressionists (Schiele, Kokoschka) rejected the "Potemkin" facade entirely. They realized that the "Beautiful Appearance" was a lie that prevented the society from confronting its own pathology. "With faith in development and in a new generation of creators and appreciators we call together all youth. As youth, we carry the future and want to create for ourselves freedom of life and of movement against the long-established older forces. Everyone who with directness and authenticity conveys that which drives him to creation, belongs to us." The distorted figures, the jarring colors, and the focus on "ugly" topics (syphilis, prostitution, mental breakdown) were expressions of the "neurasthenia" epidemic sweeping the cities.

The Internal Retreat. The collapse of the political, economic, and social order forced the Viennese intellect inward. Writers like Arthur Schnitzler began to map the psychology of the individual. His use of the internal monologue (Lieutenant Gustl, 1900) to explore the nervous mind, was a literary tool designed to expose the gap between public rank of the official and their private insecurity. The story takes place entirely within the head of a young Austrian officer who believes he has been insulted by a baker and must commit suicide to preserve his honor. The text is a chaotic stream of consciousness that reveals the "modern skepticism and despair" beneath the uniform. Gustl's thoughts are a jumble of anti-Semitic paranoia ("Must be a Jew!"), sexual anxiety, and status panic. Schnitzler exposes the "honor" of the military caste as a fragile, neurotic construct. Gustl is terrified of being "seen" or "exposed"—a perfect literary analog to the "Bassena" anxiety of the tenement dweller. The novella led to Schnitzler being stripped of his officer's commission, proving that the state considered the exposure of its psychological interior to be a treasonous act.

Prototyping Solutions to Industrialisation. Different groups tried different approaches to the central problem: industrial production operated at a scale the old craft system couldn't match, but it also produced ugliness and alienation. The Wiener Werkstätte, founded in 1903, tried to retreat. In their manifesto, they wrote: "The boundless evil caused by shoddy mass-produced goods and by uncritical imitation of old styles is like a tidal wave sweeping across the world. We have been cut adrift from the solid traditions of our forefathers and are tossed about by a thousand desires and considerations. The machine has largely replaced the hand and the businessman has supplanted the craftsman." The Werkstätte aimed to create a "Total Work of Art" (Gesamtkunstwerk) where every object—from the architecture to the cutlery—was designed with artistic unity and crafted with pre-industrial care. Their working program emphasized: "Better to work 10 days on one product than to manufacture 10 products in one day". They believed that by surrounding the modern human with honest, handmade beauty, they could counteract the alienation of the factory age. The experiment failed because it collided with the laws of the industrial economy. Craft requires time. Time, in a wage-labor economy, equals cost. A simple chair or piece of jewelry from the Werkstätte cost sums that were astronomically out of reach for the working class they theoretically championed. They aimed to save the "common man" from the ugliness of the machine, but they could only sell to the "industrial tycoon." The Werkstätte became dependent on the very class (the industrialists like the Wittgensteins, Warndorfers, and Primavesis) whose factories were destroying the world the Werkstätte tried to preserve. When the First World War and the subsequent hyperinflation destroyed the wealth of the Austro-Hungarian elite, the economic engine of the Werkstätte collapsed. It filed for bankruptcy in 1932. It was a beautiful "local optimum" that could not scale to the global reality. It attempted to solve a problem of mass society with a solution of elite scarcity.

Architecture. Adolf Loos took the opposite approach. His 1910 lecture "Ornament and Crime" made an economic argument: forcing workers to carve decoration wastes their time and health. It makes objects expensive and they get thrown out when fashions change. Just build things honestly - smooth walls, functional forms, no decoration. In 1910, Loos built the "Goldman & Salatsch" building on Michaelerplatz, directly facing the Imperial Palace. When scaffolding was removed revealing smooth-plastered upper floors without window decorations, the building police shut down construction. Emperor Franz Joseph reportedly despised it so much he avoided using the palace entrance facing the building for the rest of his life (Wien Geschichte Wiki).

Alignment: The Bauhaus and Modernism. The cycle of transition only began to resolve when a hypothesis emerged that accepted the "Material World" as it was, not as it should be. This resolution crystallized in the Bauhaus (1919–1933) and the broader Modernist movement, which stopped fighting the machine and started using it. The Modernist pivot was a shift from "How do we protect the human from the machine?" (Werkstätte) to "How do we use the machine to serve the human?" (Bauhaus). This required a "Gestalt Switch." The Bauhaus directors, such as Walter Gropius and later Hannes Meyer, accepted the industrial constraints as the new baseline for design.

An Institution of a Special Kind. When formal institutions like Parliament or the press become gridlocked or heavily censored, the social need for dialogue does not disappear. The "processing load" of society seeks the path of least resistance and often migrates to informal spaces. The Viennese coffeehouse was the laboratory for the private self and the intellectual counter-culture. The coffeehouse was already an established cultural institution. However, during this period of crisis, its function evolved. It became less of a space for pure leisure and more of a central hub for intellectual cross-pollination and information exchange (Janik & Toulmin, 1973). For the price of a single cup of coffee, a patron rented a heated, well-lit workspace for the entire day. This "renting of space" allowed for the existence of a cultural production that required no fixed capital. A writer did not need a study; an architect did not need an office; a political dissident did not need a headquarters. They needed only the price of a Melange. This lowered the barrier to entry for intellectual variance, allowing a diverse range of hypotheses to emerge from the lower economic strata. The coffeehouse functioned as a communal living room for a city that had no living rooms. The UNESCO inventory describes it as a place "where time and space are consumed, but only the coffee is found on the bill". Socially, the coffeehouse provided a mechanism for verifying sanity. When a society undergoes a paradigm shift, the individual experiences "prediction error" as personal anxiety or confusion ("Am I going crazy, or is the world breaking?"). By gathering in a communal space where speech was the primary currency, patrons could cross-reference their internal states. Zweig described them as "a sort of democratic club, open to everyone for the price of a cheap cup of coffee, where every guest can sit for hours with this little offering, to talk, write, play cards, receive post, and above all consume an unlimited number of newspapers and journals" (Zweig, 1942/2013, p. 48). Here, the "nervousness" of the age—the somatic symptom of the paradigm shift—was externalized. The "air of the coffeehouse," described by Alfred Polgar as a specific, heavy substance, was the medium in which the Viennese intellectual thrived. He described the café as a refuge for "people whose hatred of their fellow human beings is as fierce as their longing for people, who want to be alone but need companionship for it". This paradox—"alone in company"—was the essential Viennese solution to the problem of modern alienation. The social anxiety of being "left behind" acted as a forcing function, ensuring that novel or obscure concepts permeated the network far faster than they would have through traditional academic channels, as Zweig recalled: "If, for example, we discussed Nietzsche, who then was still scorned, one of us would suddenly say with feigned superiority, 'But in the idea of egotism Kierkegaard is superior to him,' and at once we became uneasy: Who is Kierkegaard, whom X knows and of whom we know nothing? The next day we stormed into the library to look up the books of this time-obscured Danish philosopher, for it was a mark of inferiority not to know some exotic thing that was familiar to someone else." These spaces had significant limits—largely male-dominated environments where women who attended faced social scrutiny, predominantly German-speaking, accessible primarily to educated middle-class men (Timms, 1986; Anderson, 2014). But within those limits, they provided places where uncertainty could be held, where competing approaches could be tested in conversation, where people could distinguish between personal failure and systemic transition. While we cannot claim the coffeehouse replaced parliament, we can argue that it served as a vital compensatory infrastructure. It provided a space where the "prediction error" of the society—the gap between official narratives and lived experience—could be collectively processed. As Janik and Toulmin (1973) argue, the intense intellectual creativity of this period was likely not a coincidence, but a direct result of this concentrated, informal exchange in the face of institutional failure.

Why it failed. The transition lasted too long. From the monarchy's collapse in 1918 to 1938, the population lived in chronic uncertainty for twenty years. Hyperinflation wiped out middle-class savings. The coffeehouses that had provided dialogue space, the artistic movements that had experimented with alternatives, the philosophical circles that had sought rigorous grounding—none proved sufficient defense when the crisis came. The cultural infrastructure remained too limited, too elite, too insular to stabilise the wider population. On March 12, 1938, Nazi Germany annexed Austria. The Klimt university paintings were destroyed in May 1945 when retreating SS forces set fire to where they'd been stored. Only black-and-white photographs survive (Natter & Frodl, 2012).

4. A modern story of change

In Vienna, we saw the common pattern of a paradigm shift: a stable social imaginary gave way under material pressures. Anomalies accumulated across demographics, politics, and psychology. A cultural search began for new ways forward—but ultimately proved insufficient against prolonged crisis. The Habsburg constructs clashed with industrial realities. Older generations clung to dynastic logic while younger ones experimented with new forms of expression and science. The transition lasted too long, the void remained unfilled, and authoritarian certainty filled the gap. Vienna showed us what a great transformation looks like. Returning to the present day, the question now for this section: Is what we are experiencing today the normal friction of being alive during any period of change, or something closer to a great transformation? The particulars of 2025 are obviously distinct from Vienna 1900. We have different technologies, different political structures, and different crises. But we now have a rough framework to tell the difference. With all we have established—the Kuhnian interplay between the material world (technology, ecology, biology) and the social world (agreements, institutions, norms); and the Human Brain that attempts to mediate between them—we can now look at our current moment and ask the same question.

Normal Culture - The Architecture of a Good Life

If Vienna's "Normal Culture" was defined by Imperial Security, what defines ours? In contemporary Western societies, the basis of the social rules and expectations we follow today were largely established in the decades following World War II. The institutions we navigate—modern welfare states, global financial systems, mass higher education, employer-sponsored healthcare, the regulatory state—were built or fundamentally restructured between 1945 and 1975. The economic logic organizing daily life—GDP growth as the primary measure of progress, consumer spending as economic engine, homeownership as wealth accumulation, credentials as gatekeepers—solidified in this same period. These structures were built by specific people responding to specific conditions. And like any structure, they carry the assumptions of their builders. Like in Zweig's case, we could say that today's Social World is most influenced by the people that built it. The most direct way to understand these assumptions is to examine the people who created them. This is why generational analysis proves useful here. Not because generations are mystical categories or because individuals within a generation think identically, but because the formative conditions of a generation—the material realities they encountered as they came of age—shape the structures they build. The generation that constructed our current institutional framework experienced childhood during the Great Depression, young adulthood during World War II, and reached positions of influence during the long postwar boom. Their structures reflect solutions to the problems they faced: preventing another economic collapse, avoiding another global war, providing basic security in a world that had demonstrated its capacity for total breakdown. They grew up assuming a certain order, perhaps one of pre-war stability or imperial certainties like in Vienna, but saw their social world collapse far beyond what they were told. They built global institutions like the UN, welfare states in Europe, and the GI Bill in the U.S, as practical ways to get food on tables, homes rebuilt, and communities knit back together. It is easy to view the structures they built—the standardized 40-hour work week, the rigid career ladder, or the centralized welfare state—with cynicism today, but these did not necessarily emerge out of bad will. They emerged as useful frameworks and attempts to stabilise a material world that had previously collapsed into war and economic ruin. As they became parents, they taught their children—our parents' generation—based on their lived experience. Having seen destruction and scarcity, they emphasized growth and stability above all. "Work hard, get educated, build something lasting"— became the lessons, drawn from a time when the alternative meant collapse. This next generation inherited the structure during its period of refinement and expansion. Their era was one of Refinement and Growth. They pushed for necessary inclusion—civil rights, women in the workforce—and embraced globalization and early digitization. The "Good Life" expanded to include travel, personal technology, and self-expression. While anomalies like the oil shocks or stagflation occurred, the underlying model held. When they taught us that "education leads to security" and "hard work leads to a home," they were not lying but reporting the facts of their lived experience. Then, came we. We, the current generation, were taught by our parents' learnings: follow the path of education to job to home, and it leads to a stable life. This felt like accumulated wisdom—millions of families over decades saying, "This worked for me, it'll work for you." A stable job meant security because it had for them; owning a home was achievable because it was in their time. However, between our grandparents and us, stood decades of change. While our grandparents struggled with scarcity and stability, we struggle with complexity and acceleration. Even with all the good will in the world, they could not have foreseen a mental health crisis born of digital abundance any more than they could have foreseen smartphones, nor the ecological challenges we now face. Technology advanced, geopolitical events unfolded, ecological pressures built, and science progressed—our knowledge grew, and our issues shifted. While the intent was always to pass down what kept them safe, the world evolved faster than the advice could update. Your dad isn't wrong that hard work built his life, but when he worked hard, a house cost three years of the average salary. Today, in many cities, it costs ten. Your teacher isn't wrong that education matters, but when she studied, a degree was a scarce asset that guaranteed a middle-class income. Now, it is a costly baseline requirement. We are the first generation where the accumulation of "more"—more education, more connectivity, more optimization—stopped to translate to "better."

Accumulating anomalies

For much of the post-war period, certain patterns held. People who obtained degrees generally found stable work. People who worked full-time could generally afford housing. Productivity improvements generally led to wage increases. The patterns were not universal, but they were consistent enough that parents could pass them down as practical guidance. These alignments allowed shared norms to solve practical problems, like coordinating work or distributing resources. Over time, however, changes in technology, ecology, and biology have created mismatches. These mismatches appear as anomalies—observations that the existing framework cannot fully explain or resolve. In Kuhn's terms, anomalies build until they signal a potential crisis, where we collectively lose trust in the old paradigm. What marks the current moment is not the presence of problems—every era has those—but the quiet accumulation of cases where old ways of life no longer hold. The standard path was: acquire credentials through education, enter the workforce, work consistently, and achieve material security. For a significant portion of people following this path, the final step no longer materializes. Groceries cost noticeably more than they did two years ago. Rent increases outpace salary adjustments. A colleague with ten years of experience can't afford to move out of their parents' house. Another has two jobs and still relies on their partner's income to cover basics. Friends who did everything "right"—good schools, stable employment, responsible financial planning—are questioning whether to have children at all. Some because the financial math simply doesn't work: childcare costs approach mortgage payments, parental leave is minimal or nonexistent, the prospect of affording both housing and child-rearing feels unrealistic. Others because they're uncertain about what world they'd be bringing a child into: climate projections, political instability, whether the social contracts they grew up with will still exist in twenty years. The cities people work in have changed visibly within a decade—new populations arriving, new languages on storefronts, cultures and norms shifting faster than previous generations experienced. Not inherently good or bad, but requiring constant adjustment, constant learning, a sense that the baseline keeps moving. This doesn't mean the old patterns have disappeared entirely. Some people still experience them working. Some regions still function as the model predicts. Some industries still deliver expected returns on investment. The system hasn't collapsed in some dramatic, visible way. It's just become inconsistent. What used to function as a reliable mechanism—perform these actions, receive these outcomes—now works sometimes, for some people, in some places. What was dependable has become probabilistic. What follows is an attempt to identify what can be observed with reasonable confidence, while acknowledging what remains uncertain.

When the Sequence Stopped Working

Anomalies today are not isolated events but interconnected patterns across domains. They stem from a core dynamic: the social world's emphasis on cognitive effort, endless growth, and optimization no longer matches a material world shaped by rapid digital acceleration, resource limits, and human biological constraints. This creates contradictions that people encounter in daily life, such as following established paths: get educated, work hard, achieve security. For previous generations, this sequence functioned as reliable guidance. A college degree opened doors that remained largely closed without one. A full-time job provided enough income for housing and savings. Hard work led, more often than not, to advancement. The sequence still exists. People still attend university, enter the workforce, attempt to buy homes. But the relationship between these steps and their expected outcomes has shifted.

Recent data shows that about half of college graduates work in positions that do not require their degree—not just immediately after graduation, but persistently. The assumption had been that this represented temporary mismatching, that people would move into degree-appropriate roles as they gained experience. For earlier generations, this largely occurred. For the current generation, a substantial portion remains in roles unrelated to their credentials a decade into their working lives. Walk into any coffee shop in a university town and you'll likely be served by someone with a bachelor's degree, possibly a master's. The barista managing your order studied engineering or communications or biology. They're not there temporarily while looking for "real" work—this is the work. The credential itself has not become worthless. Those without degrees face demonstrably worse prospects. But the degree has transformed from something that opened opportunities into something whose absence closes them. It is simultaneously necessary and insufficient—a costly baseline rather than a ladder.

In 1970, a student could cover a year's tuition at a public American university with summer work. Inflation-adjusted tuition rose from roughly $2,000 in 1980 to $10,000 by 2020. Student debt has grown from a relatively minor concern to over $1.7 trillion nationally. A student today would struggle to earn half of one semester's costs working full-time all summer. You can see this in practice: someone graduates at 22, gets their first "real job" at 30k-40k, and discovers that their minimum loan payment consumes what their parents paid in rent for a whole apartment. This creates a particular bind: you cannot afford to skip the credential, but obtaining it no longer guarantees the security it was meant to provide.

For roughly twenty-five years after World War II, productivity and compensation moved together. When companies became more efficient and workers produced more per hour, pay increased proportionally. This relationship broke sometime in the 1970s. Productivity continued its upward trajectory—accelerating, in fact, with computerization and improved processes. Wages did not follow. A software developer in 2025 can deploy an application to millions of users from their laptop in an afternoon—something that would have required teams of people and months of work in 1995. A warehouse worker processes three times as many packages per hour as they did twenty years ago, tracked and optimized by algorithms. A teacher manages larger class sizes with more administrative requirements while using digital tools that theoretically increase efficiency. The value generated per hour of work has increased dramatically. The paycheck has not increased proportionally. Growth metrics like GDP continue to rise, corporate earnings reports announce record profits, but individual security does not follow. In many Western countries, wages have tracked consumer goods inflation but not asset costs. A home that cost 3-4 years of median income in the 1980s now often requires 5-7 years or more. Check the business section of any newspaper: you'll see headlines about company valuations hitting all-time highs on the same page as articles about workers struggling to afford rent in the cities where those companies operate. This gap leaves many following the script of education and hard work yet cycling through gig jobs or short contracts. Someone works as a "consultant" because full-time positions don't exist, strings together freelance projects, drives for a rideshare service between contracts. The resume looks impressive—multiple companies, diverse experience. The reality is patching together enough income to cover basics without benefits, without predictability, without the security that full-time employment supposedly provided. Surveys show only a minority of younger workers now see single-employer careers as viable; most expect multiple income sources.

The emergence of AI adds another layer of uncertainty. It emerged from the paradigm's core logic: automate routine work to free humans for higher-order thinking. In previous waves, automation displaced manual and routine cognitive labor, and the solution was to move workers into more complex knowledge work. But if AI can perform cognitive labor more efficiently than most humans across an increasingly broad range of tasks, and if knowledge work was supposed to be the secure refuge after manufacturing automation—a graphic designer watches AI generate in seconds what would have taken them hours. A paralegal sees document review—previously a reliable source of billable hours—handled by algorithms. A junior programmer realizes the code they write to practice their skills can now be generated instantly. A copywriter discovers their client is using AI to draft everything, just wants them to "polish it." Whether this leads to widespread displacement remains debated; some projections suggest job transformation rather than loss, but the timeline and scale are not certain. What's certain is the experience of watching your specialized knowledge become suddenly less specialized, less valuable, less secure as a foundation for a career. Workers have become dramatically more efficient at generating value. That value is captured—GDP grows, corporate profits rise—but it no longer translates into equivalent increases in what workers take home. The implicit deal that structured the post-war period—that creating more value would generate more compensation for those who created it—simply stopped operating as it had. Where did the value go? Some went to returns on capital. Some to executive compensation, which grew at rates vastly exceeding median pay. Some to shareholders through buybacks and dividends. The mechanisms that previously distributed productivity gains—strong unions, rising minimum wages, tight labor markets—weakened substantially. Union representation in the private sector fell from roughly a third of workers to under one in twenty.

In 1960, the median American home cost about twice the median household's annual income. By the mid-1980s, this had risen to three and a half times. Today it stands at five times, and considerably higher in economically vibrant cities where jobs concentrate. This is not a marginal difference. For someone earning $60,000 annually, the gap between a house costing $120,000 and one costing $300,000 is the difference between achievable and prohibitive. At age 27, over half of the Silent Generation owned homes. For Baby Boomers at the same age, the figure was just under half. For Gen X, it dropped to two in five. For Millennials, one in three. The trajectory is clear and consistent. The age at which people make their first home purchase has climbed to 38—the highest recorded. The same advice parents give—work hard, save diligently, buy property to build wealth—now requires either substantially more resources, substantially more time, or substantial help from family wealth. You see this in friends who have stable jobs, good salaries, responsible spending habits—everything their parents told them would lead to homeownership—still living with roommates in their mid-thirties because saving for a down payment while paying rent is mathematically impossible. In couples where both partners have professional careers discovering they can't afford a two-bedroom apartment in the city where those careers exist. In people moving back to their childhood towns not out of preference but because it's the only place they can afford to live. At the same time rental costs have risen faster than wages, meaning a larger share of income disappears into housing costs each month, leaving less for saving toward eventual purchase or anything else. In 1960, about one in four renters spent more than 30% of their income on housing. Today, about half do. Spend time in any city and you'll hear versions of the same story: someone pays $2,000 for a one-bedroom apartment, calculates they'd need to save $60,000 for a down payment, realizes at their current savings rate that's eight years away, and that's assuming prices don't increase further.

Institutions built for stability, such as education systems, governments, media, show signs of eroding trust in polls. People increasingly report that they don't believe official sources, don't trust experts, don't think institutions are competent or acting in good faith. Depression among adolescents has risen and anxiety disorders among adults have increased substantially, doubling or near-doubling of prevalence in relatively short timeframes. Self-harm and suicide rates among young people have climbed at rates that alarm public health officials. In 2023, the U.S. Surgeon General issued an advisory noting that the health risks of social isolation rival those of smoking a pack of cigarettes daily—during what is technically the most connected era in human history. The generation with unprecedented access to educational resources, self-improvement tools, and communication technologies reports unprecedented rates of psychological distress. People across age groups report difficulty concentrating, describe feeling perpetually behind, use words like "burnt out" to describe a chronic state rather than an acute episode. Attention, which functions as a finite resource, is under constant siege. Email notifications, news alerts, social media updates, algorithmic recommendations—each individually designed to be helpful or engaging—collectively exceed the brain's capacity to process them meaningfully. The human brain evolved to handle a certain pace and volume of information. That evolutionary design has not changed. The environment has. We are, in effect, organisms adapted for one informational ecology trying to function in another. The result is not enhancement but strain. You cannot think your way out of information overload because thinking is precisely what is being overloaded. You cannot optimize your way out of optimization exhaustion because optimization is what produced the exhaustion. The paradigm assumed that economic growth and human wellbeing would move together, that optimizing for one would produce the other. For a period, particularly during the rapid postwar growth when gains were broadly shared, this correlation held well enough to seem reliable. Then it weakened. Economic indicators and lived experience began to diverge. The system continues to optimize for growth—for the metric that is measurable and has institutional backing—while the correspondence between that metric and what people actually value has become increasingly tenuous. This produces an odd situation. By the paradigm's own standards, by the numbers it uses to evaluate success, things are working. Growth continues. Productivity rises. Technology advances. But by the standards of lived experience, by what people report when asked directly about their lives, something fundamental is not delivering. The mismatch between official statistics and popular sentiment—sometimes called a "vibecession," where economic indicators say one thing and public mood says another—reflects this deeper divergence. When a system optimizes for a measurement that no longer tracks the outcome it was meant to represent, the system can appear to succeed by its own metrics while failing by the standards of those it was designed to serve. Effort decoupled from outcome. Credentials decoupled from security. Productivity decoupled from compensation. Optimization decoupled from improvement. Technologies designed to connect people decoupled from actual connection. Economic growth decoupled from broadly experienced wellbeing. The advice one generation passes to the next decoupled from the material reality in which that advice must be executed. You ask your parents what to do about your career—their advice doesn't match reality anymore. You ask experts about health, money, politics—they contradict each other. You look to institutions for guidance—they seem as confused as you are. This is what an accumulation of anomalies looks like in a social paradigm. Not dramatic, visible collapse, but the quiet proliferation of cases where predictions fail. At first, failures can be dismissed as individual misfortune, personal inadequacy, or temporary bad luck. As they multiply, they begin to seem like broader bad luck—"tough times" that will surely pass. Eventually they occur so regularly, across so many domains and for so many people, that the paradigm itself begins to appear unreliable. The question shifts from "what did I do wrong" to "does this still work." What makes this a potential crisis rather than a routine disruption is that the anomalies cannot be resolved by applying more of what the paradigm prescribes. You cannot fix the education-security problem by obtaining more education—credential inflation is part of the problem. You cannot fix the effort-outcome mismatch by working harder—overwork and burnout are symptoms of the breakdown, not solutions to it. You cannot restore the growth-wellbeing connection by generating more growth—that has been the attempted solution for decades. The tools the paradigm provides to address problems are, in many cases, the very tools that have stopped functioning. People recognize these patterns in their lived experience even when they lack formal language for them. The sense that doing everything you were supposed to do no longer guarantees anything. The feeling that requirements keep escalating while rewards diminish. The exhaustion from optimizing toward goals that recede as you approach them. The suspicion, often unarticulated but deeply felt, that the map you were given was drawn for a territory that no longer exists in quite that form.

Common Patterns

Our anomalies today span the economic, the psychological, and the technological, but they share a pattern: a mismatch between the constructs we inherit and the biological and material reality we actually inhabit. On the macro level: our economic and political systems assume infinite growth on a finite planet, continuous productivity increases, indefinite extraction of resources. Ecological limits make these assumptions increasingly difficult to maintain. On the micro level: our daily environments demand more top-down processing, more abstract coordination, more cognitive load than our evolutionary biology was built to handle. We routinely manage abstractions with no direct sensory correlate: credit scores that determine where you can live, follower counts that affect career opportunities, algorithmic rankings that control what information reaches you, news about conflicts in places you've never visited but are expected to have informed opinions about. The brain, evolved for small-group coordination in physical environments, now navigates global-scale coordination in digital ones. This is not entirely new. We have known for decades that industrialized life creates friction with our evolutionary inheritance—that's why we sleep poorly under artificial light, why we crave sugar our ancestors rarely found, why sitting at desks produces back pain. Vienna at the turn of the twentieth century already faced this tension. Freud developed psychoanalysis partly in response to the psychological strain of urban industrial life. The Secessionists arose from a sense that something in the new material conditions was incompatible with human needs. The post-war paradigm inherited this mismatch, attempted to manage it, and in some ways intensified it. The dominant response has been cognitive: we try to think our way through it. More analysis, more information, more optimization. Feeling stressed? Read a book about stress management. Overwhelmed by choices? Develop a decision-making framework. Can't sleep? Download a sleep tracking app and analyze your patterns. Recall the framework established earlier. Societies function through the interaction of three domains: the material world (resources, technology, ecological limits), the social world (institutions, norms, agreements), and the human organism that mediates between them. When these three align reasonably well, the system feels functional. When misalignments accumulate, friction emerges. The friction we observe concentrates around a specific pattern: contemporary life requires managing more abstractions, more simultaneous mental models of things not directly present, than previous eras demanded. When strain appears, the paradigm's typical response is to prescribe more of what created it—more analysis, more information processing, more cognitive management.

The Abstraction Gradient

Since the Enlightenment, Western culture has placed thinking on a pedestal. When Descartes famously said, "I think, therefore I am," he positioned rational thought as the foundation of existence. This emphasis on reason was a necessary tool; it produced the scientific revolution, democratic institutions, and the technologies that define modern life. Thinking is perhaps our species' greatest capability. However, as the scale of human coordination expanded—stretching across borders, languages, and generations—the layer of abstraction necessarily thickened. To enable cooperation between millions of strangers, we built vast systems of law, finance, and data. The cumulative result is a shift in habitat. We have constructed a world where the density of our agreements now outweighs the density of our physical interactions. We moved from a life defined by the manipulation of matter to a life defined by the processing of information. Psychiatrist Iain McGilchrist argues in The Master and His Emissary that Western culture has become increasingly dominated by left-hemisphere modes of thinking—analytical, categorical, abstract, and detached from embodied context. The left hemisphere excels at manipulation and control, at breaking wholes into parts that can be measured and managed. When this mode operates without the right hemisphere's attention to lived experience, relationship, and the whole, the result is a world that feels fragmented and alienated even as it becomes more technically sophisticated. Someone living in a small agricultural community managed perhaps 150 relationships with people they saw regularly, seasonal cycles they could observe directly, and work whose results were visible and immediate. When you planted seeds, you saw the crop. The connection between effort and outcome was tangible and local. Someone in 1950 navigated a broader scope—their local community, regional news, a job that might involve some abstraction, family networks extending beyond the immediate household. But most information still had relatively direct connection to lived experience. You might read about national politics in a newspaper once a day, but the bulk of what occupied your attention related to people and places you could physically encounter. Today you're expected to maintain awareness of global conflicts you cannot affect, economic indicators that may or may not impact your job security, social media dynamics across multiple platforms where your professional reputation is somehow at stake, algorithmic rankings that determine whether your work gets seen, professional networks spanning continents with people you've never met face-to-face but are supposed to "maintain relationships" with, news cycles updating by the minute about events on the other side of the world that you're expected to have informed opinions about. Our globalized world and digital infrastructure require us to keep exponentially more abstract constructs active simultaneously—constructs with no direct sensory correlate, no immediate physical presence. You're supposed to care about your credit score (a number calculated by algorithms based on financial behavior), maintain your social media presence (an abstract representation of your identity), optimize your professional network (relationships that exist primarily as database entries), stay informed about global events (things happening to people you'll never meet in places you'll never go), all while actually doing your job, maintaining your physical health, and having relationships with people you can actually see. This environment creates a specific behavioral trap, particularly for younger generations. We have built educational and digital systems that reward the manipulation of symbols (grades, likes, comments) but offer fewer opportunities for physical agency. This can lead to a condition of getting "lost in the world of signs and forgetting the things they signify." We risk confusing the map with the territory. We may confuse the symbol of achievement (the Grade, the Status) with the reality of it (the Learning, the Work). When the brain is locked in a loop of pure abstraction, it loses the ability to distinguish between a thought about a problem and the reality of the problem. This produces a particular form of learned helplessness. When you act in the physical world—build something, fix something, grow something—you get immediate feedback. It works or it doesn't. You see why and can adjust. But when you're optimizing abstract metrics, you can do everything "right" and still fail because the connection between your actions and outcomes has been severed by layers of abstraction you can't control or even see. When most of your day involves top-down processing—maintaining mental models of things not physically present, managing abstractions, making decisions about representations—with limited time for the automatic, sensory-grounded processing the brain handles more easily, the system experiences strain.

Patterns of Strain

This strain shows up in predictable ways. Attention becomes harder to sustain. People report difficulty concentrating, feeling mentally clouded, a sense of operating through fog. Clinically, this represents a form of dissociation. When the brain becomes overwhelmed by the volume and complexity of abstract processing it's being asked to maintain, it begins to dissociate as a protective mechanism. The system is overloaded, so it partially disconnects. Research on anxiety suggests it functions as a failure of the prediction mechanism—anxious individuals often hold what researchers call "hyper-rigid priors," maintaining predictions of danger even when the immediate environment is safe. The brain prioritizes the internal model over external reality. The dominant diagnoses of our time—Generalized Anxiety Disorder, OCD, Depressive Rumination—share a common structure: the brain getting stuck in recursive loops of abstract processing. The anxious person running endless simulations of future threats. The depressed person trapped in rumination about past failures. The person with OCD unable to trust direct sensory experience over the mental model demanding "just one more check." These conditions have biological substrates—measurable differences in brain structure, neurotransmitter function, genetic predisposition. But they're also patterns that an environment demanding constant abstract processing, continuous prediction of distant futures, and management of endless what-if scenarios is well-designed to trigger and reinforce. When rates of a condition increase dramatically within a single generation, we're not looking at a genetic shift. We're looking at an environment that increasingly triggers vulnerabilities that were always present but previously remained dormant. Think of it like allergies: the immune systems haven't fundamentally changed, but different environmental exposures produce different outcomes.

Coping Mechanisms Across Time

How people cope with psychological strain has varied across generations, largely determined by the material conditions they faced. Someone born in the 1940s grew up in a world still recovering from massive instability—economic depression, world war, fundamental uncertainty about survival. For that generation, teaching children to work hard, to be resilient, to not dwell on difficulties made practical sense. Material conditions demanded it. You couldn't afford to process everything you felt because you had to work, had to survive, had to build. The coping mechanisms that developed—compartmentalization, forward focus, emotional restraint—were adaptations to genuine constraints. When you have to work full-time to provide basics, when mental health resources don't exist or are deeply stigmatized, when showing vulnerability could cost you employment or social standing, you develop protective mechanisms that allow you to continue functioning. Later generations faced different material conditions. More economic stability allowed different possibilities. The development of clinical psychology and psychiatry—however imperfect—created new options. Knowledge accumulated about how childhood experiences affect adult functioning, how emotional patterns form, what interventions might help. This created different coping strategies. Not better or worse in some absolute sense, but different—adapted to different constraints and different available resources.

The Development of Psychological Knowledge. Mental health and therapy are contentious topics for legitimate reasons. The field has a complicated history. Early psychoanalysis induced false memories in patients, leading to waves of unfounded accusations and genuine harm. Overprescription of psychiatric medication remains a serious problem. Many therapists are poorly trained. The commercialization of mental health has created problematic incentives. However, at its best, contemporary clinical psychology represents accumulated knowledge about how human psychology functions—knowledge derived from decades of research, measurement, clinical observation, and refinement. Current therapeutic frameworks identify patterns in how people develop coping mechanisms in childhood and how these mechanisms affect adult functioning. When a child encounters situations that exceed their capacity to process, they develop protective mechanisms to continue functioning. A child who learns to suppress emotional expression in a household where emotions aren't safe is developing a functional survival strategy for that environment. The issue arises when these protective mechanisms, adaptive in one context, persist into contexts where they no longer serve. The person who learned to suppress all emotional vulnerability as a child may struggle to form intimate relationships as an adult. The person who learned to tie their self-worth entirely to achievement may experience profound distress when they inevitably encounter failure. Different generations developed different protective mechanisms because they faced different childhood environments, shaped by different material conditions. For someone raised in the 1950s, when economic stability demanded constant work and emotional expression was viewed as weakness, developing mechanisms of emotional compartmentalization made practical sense. For someone raised in the 2000s, when economic stability was less certain but emotional expression was more accepted, different mechanisms emerged.

Material Conditions and Parenting. Parents across all generations have generally wanted the best for their children and worked with the knowledge and resources available to them. This is worth stating plainly because discussions of childhood development can easily slide into implicit blame. Raising children is difficult under any circumstances, and our world is not designed to support it well. Parents typically work full-time, often in cognitively demanding jobs. They navigate their own psychological patterns, formed in their own childhoods. They operate in economic systems that provide minimal support for caregiving. They receive conflicting advice about what constitutes good parenting, often lacking clear frameworks or adequate time to figure it out. In this context, mistakes are inevitable. Not moral failures—statistical inevitabilities given the constraints. What constitutes "good parenting" has shifted as knowledge has accumulated. A parent in 1960 operating according to the best available understanding of child development would make different choices than a parent in 2020 with access to decades of additional research on attachment, emotional regulation, and developmental psychology. Knowledge accumulates. Understanding of infection improved after germ theory. Understanding of child development has similarly improved.

Generational Patterns

The generation that experienced significant childhood instability during the Depression and war years tended to emphasize performance, achievement, and emotional resilience in raising their own children. This made sense given their experience—these were the traits that enabled survival and success in their world. However, research on personality development suggests that when a child's sense of worth becomes tightly tied to performance—when parental approval is conditional on achievement—this correlates with increased rates of certain personality patterns in adulthood. In clinical terms: narcissistic patterns (more common in men) and histrionic patterns (more common in women) both often trace to childhood environments where love felt conditional on performance. This is not an accusation. Parents who tied approval to achievement typically believed they were helping their children succeed. They were operating with the best available knowledge in their context. The next generation inherited both the material stability their parents built and the psychological patterns their parents' coping mechanisms produced. And they faced a new complication: the strategies that worked for their parents—work hard, achieve, perform—were encountering diminishing returns as material conditions shifted. When credentials no longer guarantee stability, when working hard no longer ensures housing affordability, when the social contract that promised "perform well and you'll be secure" begins to break down, those raised to stake their worth on performance experience particular strain. This creates tension not because either generation is wrong, but because the same coping mechanism that was adaptive in one set of material conditions becomes maladaptive when those conditions change.

There's a common argument: younger generations report more mental health issues because we've created categories and frameworks for them. In previous generations, people just "got on with it" without diagnosis or therapy. Therefore, the increase in reported mental health problems reflects measurement artifact, not genuine increase in suffering. This argument resembles saying that infectious disease became more common after germ theory was discovered. Germ theory didn't create infection—it revealed what was always happening and provided tools to address it. The discovery of germs didn't make the past better by virtue of people not knowing about them. Ignorance of a mechanism doesn't prevent the mechanism from operating. Our increased capacity to identify and measure psychological patterns doesn't create those patterns. What's changed is visibility and vocabulary. Previous generations experienced depression, anxiety, trauma responses—they simply lacked frameworks to understand these as coherent phenomena with identifiable causes rather than personal failings or mysterious afflictions. This matters because humans make decisions, and humans in positions of power are humans with the full range of human psychological patterns. When we examine current problems—political dysfunction, short-term decision-making that ignores long-term consequences, inability to update models when evidence contradicts them—we can trace causes through three interconnected domains: Material constraints: Finite resources, ecological limits, physical infrastructure. Psychological patterns: The cognitive and emotional patterns of the humans designing and operating systems, from normal human biases to diagnosable conditions. Incentive structures: The institutional arrangements that reward certain behaviors and punish others, creating pressures that can override individual judgment. These three interact. A leader with untreated narcissistic patterns (psychological) operating in a corporate structure that rewards quarterly gains over long-term stability (incentives) while managing genuinely finite resources (material) will produce different outcomes than if any of these factors were different. Previous generations weren't operating with better mental health—they were operating without frameworks to identify or address psychological patterns that affected decision-making. The executive making impulsive, ego-driven choices in 1975 experienced the same underlying patterns as one in 2025. The difference is that now we can sometimes identify the pattern, name it, and potentially address it rather than treating it as simply "leadership style" or "strong personality." This isn't about assigning blame. People work with the knowledge available to them. But when knowledge accumulates—when we understand that certain psychological patterns produce predictable decision-making failures, that childhood experiences shape adult capacity in measurable ways, that emotional regulation affects judgment—pretending that knowledge doesn't exist doesn't make it stop being true. The increased visibility of mental health in younger generations isn't weakness. It's the application of accumulated knowledge about how human psychology actually functions. Whether that knowledge gets used effectively is a separate question. But the knowledge itself represents progress in the same way that understanding infection represents progress over attributing disease to bad air or moral failing.

The Response to Strain

When people experience this strain, the paradigm offers solutions that follow its core logic. Feeling overwhelmed? Use better time management systems. Can't focus? Try productivity techniques. Anxious? Analyze your thought patterns using cognitive frameworks. Burnt out? Optimize your self-care routine. These approaches help many people. They're not wrong. But notice their structure: they respond to cognitive strain by prescribing more cognitive effort. They ask you to think about your thinking, plan your planning, manage your management. This can work up to a point. But it also potentially adds another layer of abstraction to track and maintain. When housing becomes expensive and difficult to access, when physical resources grow scarce, when planetary boundaries become visible, one response is to expand the domain where constraints don't apply: the realm of pure representation. Digital currencies that exist only as recorded agreements. Virtual real estate in online environments. Digital goods and assets. Platforms built around infinite scroll and unlimited content. These aren't necessarily problems in themselves—they serve real functions and provide real value to many people. But they reflect a broader tendency: when the physical world becomes difficult or expensive to navigate, create more space in the virtual world where scarcity can be designed out. This makes pragmatic sense in many contexts. If you cannot afford physical space, virtual space is a reasonable alternative. If physical travel is impossible, virtual connection is better than no connection. The pattern is noteworthy not because it's irrational but because it represents the paradigm operating consistently with its core logic: when you hit limits in one domain, expand in the domain of representations and abstractions. This strain is most visible in the generation that treats the anomalies as a baseline - the younger generations. We see how they try to deal with them in therapy, in memes, in their social media behavior, in their longing for community and how they view AI.

The Infamous Generation Z

Painting a picture of this generation is challenging. Their responses are fragmented, contradictory, and spread across platforms that older generations often cannot access. How do you explain the cultural and psychological significance of their meme culture to someone who's never encountered it? How do you describe their relationship with technology, work, and meaning in language that's universally understandable rather than dismissive or celebratory? Similar to Viennese coffeehouses in 1900, this generation uses digital infrastructure as their "communal living room." But where Vienna's coffeehouses offered refuge from imperial rigidity, these digital spaces offer refuge from cities that feel overwhelming in their solitude and busyness. Comment sections, group chats, algorithmic feeds function as a distributed coffeehouse. Here, the nervousness of the age is externalized. They use these spaces to be "alone in company"—physically isolated but socially integrated. Behind layers of dark humor, performative detachment, and "lobotomy chic", an earnest, secret search for meaning. "Life of someone born in 2000: Pandemic at the age of enjoyment. AI implementation at the age of employment." The most visible—and most frequently misunderstood—aspect of Gen Z culture is its reliance on meme formats to process collective experience. To older generations, these often appear as frivolous, nihilistic, or deliberately obscure. But examined through the lens we've established, memes function as a distributed sense-making apparatus operating at the speed required by their conditions. A meme is, structurally, a compressed philosophical statement paired with a template for rapid iteration. When someone posts "the only two options are: become so cringe that you're based again, or become so based that you're cringe again," they are articulating—in highly compressed form—the collapse of stable status hierarchies and the impossibility of authentic self-presentation in a performance economy. The statement is absurd, yes, but it precisely captures a real tension that formal language struggles to express. The aesthetic movements that dominate Gen Z's platforms reveal the psychological pattern we would expect during paradigm crisis: oscillation between despair and defiant meaning-making. Corecore emerged when creator @heksensabbat used the hashtag in July 2022, though the format traces to Mason Noel's January 2021 video. By March 2023, #corecore had surpassed 2.1 billion views. The format involves collaging disparate clips—news footage of disasters, film scenes of existential breakdown, TikTokers scrolling endlessly, melting ice caps. Hyperallergic compared it to Dadaism: "Both movements attribute meaning to the meaningless." TechCrunch described the "techno-futurism-doom vibes" as "tied together by a general malaise—a concern that life has no meaning and technology is alienating us from one another." Hopecore emerged as corecore's counterweight. Key creator Zaid Khan (@zaidleppelin) launched his "resist the dread" series in November 2022; Carolyn Fan developed anti-nihilism TikTok content. The hashtag accumulated 8 million+ likes. Where corecore documents alienation, hopecore deploys inspirational sports clips, video game scenes, and quotes from Albert Camus—particularly "In the depth of winter, I finally learned that within me there lay an invincible summer." The format uses identical editing techniques but toward affirmation rather than despair. Viewer responses: "We're all going to make it." Researchers call it "optimistic nihilism": accepting meaninglessness but choosing to create meaning anyway—a Camusian position where "one must imagine Sisyphus happy." Their relationship to AI captures the broader paradox: Gen Z is simultaneously the most AI-fluent generation and the most economically vulnerable to AI disruption. They mock ChatGPT's failures while using it daily; they organize protests against AI art while some hope for a concept called the Singularity, AI replacing jobs so humans can… They are neither accelerationists nor decelerationists but pragmatists navigating contradictions. Gen Z reports higher rates of anxiety, depression, and psychological distress than previous generations at the same age. This is often interpreted as evidence of their fragility. But recall the earlier analysis: increased measurement doesn't create the phenomenon. Germ theory didn't create infection; it made infection visible and addressable. Gen Z didn't invent mental health problems. They inherited them—both the genetic and environmental factors that produce psychological strain, and crucially, the knowledge to identify and name these patterns. Where previous generations experienced these same conditions as personal failings or mysterious afflictions, Gen Z has vocabulary: anxiety, depression, ADHD, trauma responses, nervous system dysregulation.

Common Patterns

Economic constructs assuming infinite growth meet finite planetary boundaries. Work structures expecting continuous cognitive performance produce measurable strain when they don't account for what depletes or restores attention. Social scripts promising stability through credentials were built for material conditions that have since shifted. Digital infrastructure optimized for engagement metrics affects nervous system regulation in ways we can now measure. The common pattern: treating bounded systems as if they were unbounded. Assuming that what works in the abstract will work in physical reality. The frameworks were designed with incomplete information about how bodies, minds, and ecosystems actually function. These assumptions were not unreasonable when formed—they reflected the best available understanding and delivered real progress in many areas. The issue is that evidence has accumulated suggesting the assumptions are incomplete. We now know more about attention depletion and recovery, what built environments support or strain biological regulation, how cognitive load compounds, where ecological boundaries lie. The knowledge exists. The question is whether systems integrate this knowledge into how they operate, or continue optimizing based on assumptions that no longer fully match observed reality. This is not about whether modern life is "good" or "bad." It's about fit and sustainability. Tools and approaches that opened genuine possibilities in one context may work less well when extended indefinitely. The paradigm's core mechanisms—expanding abstract coordination, increasing efficiency, maximizing measured outputs—solved real problems. They may simply have been applied past their optimal dosage in certain domains.

Crisis or not

We now have found a lot of anomalies across economic, psychological, and technological domains. Among them, we see a common pattern: once again, the material and social world have drifted too far apart and the social world we live in doesn't seem to match the reality we're experiencing in our day-to-day life. What we were taught about education and careers does not align with the actual job market. Economic models assume conditions that have shifted. Daily environments demand more from our biology than it was built to handle. This brings us back to the question we set out to answer - to distinguish subjective experiences of change from broader societal transformations. To do so, we have drawn on Thomas Kuhn's framework from the history of science, extending it to cultural and social realms. In Kuhn's model, anomalies accumulate until a paradigm enters crisis, at which point a search for new forms of meaning begins. The question now is whether we are already in crisis. This is hard to say. As we established earlier, Kuhn developed his framework for scientific communities—bounded groups with clear training and consensus mechanisms. Cultural domains lack these features. Diagnosis from within is difficult. What feels like a crisis to one part of society may feel like normal friction to another. But no matter whether we're in a crisis or not, just as in Vienna, we can see that amidst the anomalies, there's already searches for new forms emerging. These are still very fragmented, spread across millions of people and not easily "categorisable" - which will be our task at hand in this next section. We previously said, that change of any kind affects our lives. A lot of people reading this, while hopefully intrigued, will therefore ultimately not care if this moment will later classify as a great transformation. Whether paradigm change or not, if these anomalies are real, people will try to find ways to deal with them and adapt to the conditions they face. If the core problem is that what you were taught about how life works no longer matches what you're actually experiencing, then we should see people looking for what actually does work. How an individual deals with the rising housing costs, job market, or mental health issues, might tell us a lot about how we could address these anomalies on a bigger scale. Therefore, the most productive place to look for these adaptations is where they correlate with the anomalies themselves. For physical and biological mismatches, we can examine what medicine and clinical practice have learned to help their patients. For psychological strain, we can look at what therapy and psychiatry reveal about what restores balance. For cultural friction, we can observe how expression and art are processing the transformation. For technological change, we can study how previous generations integrated fundamentally new tools into their lives.

Glimpses of an Emerging Paradigm

When anomalies accumulate in a paradigm, people search for corrections. This search does not wait for permission. It is not coordinated. It emerges from millions of individual encounters with friction—people trying things because what they were doing stopped working.

The search is already underway. It takes many forms, and not all of them point in the same direction.

Some people accelerate toward technology. They reason that previous technological disruptions eventually created more abundance than scarcity, and they expect this pattern to continue. They follow researchers working on artificial intelligence, hoping automation will eliminate tedious work and free humans to pursue what they find meaningful. They invest time in digital spaces—virtual worlds, digital assets, online communities—partly because physical space in desirable cities has become prohibitively expensive. When the material world presents constraints, they expand into spaces where constraints can be designed differently. The push for the "Metaverse," the expansion of digital currency, and the architecture of the "infinite scroll" are attempts to create infinite territory for the mind because the territory for the body feels constrained.

Others move in the opposite direction. They experience digital life as depleting rather than liberating, and they pull back toward the physical and analog. Vinyl record sales have surpassed CD sales for the first time in decades. People buy phones that only make calls and send texts, deliberately limiting their own connectivity. Running clubs in cities have waiting lists. Community gardens have more applicants than plots. Interest in fermentation, woodworking, ceramics, and other manual practices has grown visibly.

Neither direction is wrong. Both are experiments. In Kuhn's framework, this is what crisis looks like—the community splinters and tries different approaches simultaneously. What is notable is not that any single approach has won, but that the search itself is so widespread. The old answers are no longer sufficient for enough people that millions are trying to draw new maps.

Beneath this variety, however, certain patterns appear in what helps.

What Clinical Practice Reveals

The most common psychological conditions of our time—generalized anxiety, panic disorder, OCD, depressive rumination—share a structural feature. In each case, the brain's predictive model has become stuck in a recursive loop, maintaining an internal narrative that overrides sensory evidence.

A person experiencing a panic attack feels certain something terrible is happening. Their heart races. They cannot breathe properly. But the sensory evidence contradicts the internal model—the room is safe, the air is breathable, there is no threat. The prediction has detached from reality and is running on its own momentum.

The clinical intervention used in hospitals and therapy offices is direct. It does not ask the person to think differently. It reconnects them to their senses.

Name five things you can see. Four things you can hear. Three things you can touch. Two things you can smell. One thing you can taste.

The brain is running a top-down loop that has detached from bottom-up input. Strengthening the signal from the senses interrupts the loop.

Exposure therapy for OCD follows the same principle. A person feels compelled to check the door repeatedly because their mental model insists it might be unlocked, even after checking. The therapy gradually teaches them to trust direct sensory evidence over the compulsive prediction. You checked the door. It is locked. You can see that it is locked. The prediction is wrong.

Treatment for dissociation brings attention back to the body. Where are your feet? What do you feel against your skin? The person has become untethered from sensory experience. The intervention re-anchors them.

Depression and anxiety disorders often involve rumination—the mind running the same abstract patterns repeatedly, detached from what is actually happening in the immediate environment. Effective interventions include physical activity, time in nature, and practices that anchor attention in the present moment. These work not through insight or analysis but through shifting the balance between internal prediction and external input.

The common thread: when the brain's predictive model becomes stuck in abstract loops, the most reliable way out involves reconnecting to direct sensory experience. Not thinking differently about the problem. Encountering the physical world directly enough that the prediction system recalibrates.

The Same Pattern, Outside the Clinic

This mechanism is not confined to clinical settings. The same pattern—rebalancing abstraction with direct experience—appears across domains where people are independently trying to address the strain described in earlier sections.

People report that going for a walk helps them think more clearly. That working with their hands—building something, cooking, repairing—resolves mental strain that purely cognitive rest does not. That time in nature restores a capacity for attention that indoor environments deplete. These are not just preferences. They reflect something consistent about how the system functions.

The internet meme "touch grass," which urges people to leave their screens and physically go outside, is folk neuroscience. It is imprecise, but it points at something real. When the internal model has become tangled in abstract loops—political anxiety, social comparison, information overload—physical contact with the world outside the head can reset the system.

This pattern is visible at larger scales. Fitness culture has shifted from aesthetics toward function—not how the body looks but how it actually works. Metabolic health, sleep architecture, nervous system regulation have moved from specialist concerns to mainstream conversation. Scientists like Andrew Huberman reach millions of viewers explaining practical neuroscience. Meditation apps have normalized practices that were previously niche. Wearable devices track heart rate variability and sleep quality as standard features. The mindfulness economy reached $2.1 billion globally in 2022.

Meanwhile, interest in somatic therapy—approaches that work through the body rather than through talk—has grown substantially. Practices that emphasize bodily awareness, breathwork, and physical co-regulation have moved from alternative margins toward clinical mainstream. The growth of trauma-informed approaches in psychology, which recognize that trauma is stored in the body and must be addressed somatically rather than purely cognitively, reflects accumulated evidence about how the system actually heals.

At the same time, the dominant culture continues to prescribe more of what created the strain. Feeling overwhelmed? Use better time management systems. Cannot focus? Try productivity techniques. Anxious? Analyze your thought patterns using cognitive frameworks. Burnt out? Optimize your self-care routine. Notice the structure: these respond to cognitive strain by prescribing more cognitive effort. They ask you to think about your thinking, plan your planning, manage your management.

The two tendencies coexist. The paradigm's own logic continues to operate while corrections emerge from within it. This is consistent with what Kuhn described—during crisis, the old paradigm does not simply stop. It continues alongside the experiments that will eventually replace it.

Being Present

The state where the brain's two streams—prediction and sensation—come into balance has a name in ordinary language. We call it being present.

Being present does not mean eliminating thought. It does not mean living only in the immediate sensory moment. It means the internal model and external reality are aligned. You are where you are. Your predictions match your circumstances. The map fits the territory.

This state has observable properties. Attention can focus without constant effort. The mind does not pull elsewhere. Decisions feel grounded because they are based in actual conditions rather than abstract simulation. Action is possible because the gap between intention and execution is small. Rest is restorative because the system actually recovers rather than remaining vigilant while pretending to rest.

Being present is not a permanent achievement. It is a state that depends on conditions—partly on individual practice, partly on environmental support. A person can be present in one environment and unable to be present in another. A room with natural light, reasonable acoustic conditions, and human-scale proportions makes this state easier to achieve than a room with fluorescent lighting, constant notification sounds, and no windows. This is not philosophy. It is measurable.

If being present represents a functional baseline for human organisms—the state in which the system operates as designed—and if contemporary environments make this state increasingly difficult to achieve, then the task is not only individual practice but the creation of conditions that support this state at scale.

Completing Modernism

The Modernist architects and designers of the early twentieth century rebelled against historicism. They rejected ornament, tradition, and decoration. They championed function, efficiency, and rationality. Their principle: "Form follows function."

This was not wrong. It was incomplete.

Modernism optimized for measurable function—how efficiently a building housed workers, how quickly a factory produced goods, how cheaply materials could be used. It created genuine progress. The Bauhaus resolved the Wiener Werkstätte's failure by accepting industrial production rather than fighting it, asking how the machine could serve the human rather than how the human could be protected from the machine. This was the correct move. It accepted the material world as it was.

But it left something out.

A building can function efficiently by all measurable standards and still produce dissociation in the people who occupy it. A workplace can maximize productivity and generate burnout. A technology can solve problems and fragment attention. A city can house millions and feel isolating to most of them.

When Modernists designed schools with fluorescent lighting, no windows, and hard acoustic surfaces, they optimized for cost and maintenance. They did not account for how these conditions affect attention, nervous system regulation, and learning capacity. The building functioned by the metric of housing students. It failed by the metric of supporting learning. The metric was incomplete, not the intention.

Function without accounting for human biological and psychological needs is incomplete function. We now have substantial evidence—from chronobiology, environmental psychology, acoustic engineering, and clinical neuroscience—about what conditions support or degrade the human organism's capacity to function. Natural light affects circadian rhythms. Ceiling height affects cognitive flexibility. Acoustic environments affect processing load. Material textures affect sensory regulation. These are not preferences. They are measurable variables.

The full sequence is not "form follows function" but "form follows function follows feeling."

This is not a call to add decoration. It is not a retreat from Modernism. It is its completion. The Modernist project asked: does this work? The completed version asks: does this work, and does it support the organism that uses it? The second question does not replace the first. It extends it.

The principle applies beyond architecture. An education system optimized purely for credential delivery produces the pattern described in earlier sections—people with degrees but without applicable understanding or capacity. A healthcare system optimized for treating acute conditions fails at chronic prevention. A social media platform optimized for engagement produces the psychological patterns documented earlier. A work culture optimized for productivity generates burnout. In each case, the optimization succeeded by its own metric and failed by the metric it did not include.

Contemporary architecture and design have begun to reintegrate what was left out. Biophilic design incorporates natural elements not as decoration but as functional infrastructure for attention and regulation. Acoustic engineering considers sensory comfort alongside sound isolation. Lighting design accounts for circadian biology. These corrections are not luxuries. They are the application of accumulated knowledge about how the system actually works.

The Integration Problem

We have transforming technologies—AI most visibly—but lack cultural integration. This is not a new pattern.

László Moholy-Nagy, a core member of the Bauhaus who later founded the New Bauhaus in Chicago, observed this dynamic in the 1940s. In his book Vision in Motion, he describes how designers responded to a material revolution in their everyday craft. For centuries, tool handles had been made of wood, shaped on a lathe. The lathe constrained what shapes were possible—round, symmetrical, following the logic of rotation.

Then plastic molding arrived. Steel dies could produce any shape. The constraint was gone. Yet for years, molded plastic handles looked exactly like the old wooden ones.

Moholy-Nagy identified the error: "Industrial designers and producers of goods often make the mistake of ignoring this axiom. For example, although the steel dies for mass-produced plastic moldings are different in characteristics from the lathe, which produced the 'typical' old wooden handle for tools, the new molded plastic tool handles still look like the old wooden ones."

The designer "did not understand the changed facilities for mass production. He unconsciously retarded progress in foisting obsolete lathe-shapes upon the new material."

It was not malicious or lazy. The designers could not imagine what else a handle could be. The constraint of the lathe had shaped their entire conception of form. Even when the constraint was removed, the mental model persisted. They were making plastic that looked like wood because wood was all they knew.

The breakthrough came when enough time and experimentation passed that designers understood the material's actual properties—that it could be molded to fit the hand ergonomically, that it did not need to be round, that form could follow function in entirely new ways. The old form was not wrong for its material. It was wrong for the new one.

The same pattern is observable now. We use AI to generate marketing campaigns that look like manually produced ones. We use it to write emails in the same format we wrote them by hand. We ask it to produce paintings in the style of existing painters, compose music in existing genres, write screenplays following existing structures. The question being asked is: how can this new tool replicate what the old tool did? The question not yet being asked, at least not widely, is: what can this tool do that was not possible before? What forms are native to its actual properties?

This is not a criticism. It is a description of where we are in the integration process. Moholy-Nagy's observation suggests this phase is normal. New tools are always first used to replicate old outputs. Cultural processing—millions of small experiments with the new material, testing what works, discovering what resonates—is what eventually reveals the native forms. That processing takes time. It cannot be rushed by the companies producing the tools. It happens in studios, classrooms, living rooms, conversations.

This is where expression matters again. Not as decoration or entertainment, but as the mechanism through which a society discovers what its new tools are actually for. Every experiment—a filmmaker testing what AI-assisted editing enables, a musician exploring sounds that were previously impossible, a designer prototyping forms that only generative tools can produce—contributes to the collective understanding of what the material can do. Most experiments will fail or prove trivial. Some will reveal genuine possibilities. The variance is the process.

Local as Laboratory

If the corrections described above point in a consistent direction—toward rebalancing abstraction with experience, toward completing function with feeling, toward culturally integrating new technologies rather than merely deploying them—then the question becomes: where does this work happen?

There is a tendency for answers to come from centralized sources—large technology companies, national governments, international institutions. These entities have resources, expertise, and reach. They can implement at scale.

But they face a structural limitation. They are too far from the specific contexts where their answers must work. An AI company in San Francisco, however capable, cannot determine what integration of their technology should look like in Viennese schools. A national education policy cannot account for the specific needs of each community. Solutions designed at scale tend to optimize for measurable uniformity rather than contextual fit.

Not every question requires a technological solution or a policy intervention. What is the role of masculinity in a changing world? That is a cultural question. It will be answered not by an app but through the stories people tell, the behaviors they model in their communities, the conversations they have in their friend groups. How should a neighborhood integrate new populations? That is a local question. It will be answered not by national policy alone but by whether people in that neighborhood have spaces to encounter each other directly.

Local is where culture happens before it becomes Culture. Where experiments can succeed or fail without requiring institutional permission or venture capital. Where someone can test whether a different way of organizing work feels better. Where spaces can be built that serve human sensory needs rather than pure efficiency metrics. Where people can gather and try forms of coordination not entirely mediated through screens.

The Viennese coffeehouse served this function during the last major transition. For the price of a single cup of coffee, a patron rented a heated, well-lit workspace for an entire day. A writer did not need a study; an architect did not need an office; a dissident did not need a headquarters. The infrastructure lowered the barrier to entry for intellectual variance. It allowed a diverse range of approaches to emerge from people who could not have afforded formal institutional access.

Movements like the Secession, the Wiener Werkstätte, the Bauhaus, and the philosophical circles that formed in Viennese coffeehouses were all local experiments before they became historical reference points. They began as small groups of people trying to make sense of a changing world in a specific place. Some of their experiments failed—the Werkstätte could not scale its craft model to industrial reality. Some succeeded—the Bauhaus resolved the tension between craft and machine in ways that shaped a century of design. The common feature was that they started locally, with the resources available, and addressed the questions that mattered in their immediate context.

If the current transition follows a similar pattern—and the parallels, while imperfect, are substantial—then we should expect the most productive experiments to emerge not from centralized planning but from local initiative. People in a specific place, dealing with specific friction, trying specific approaches, and sharing what works.

Social media and digital communication have added a dimension that did not exist during Vienna's transition: local experiments that prove useful can be communicated globally almost immediately. A community model tested in one city can be studied and adapted by another within weeks rather than decades. The coffeehouse took centuries to spread across Europe. A successful local format today can spread in months. This does not replace the local work—the experiment still has to happen somewhere specific, with real people, in a real room. But it means that local is no longer synonymous with isolated.

The creation of local cultural infrastructure—spaces where people can gather, express, experiment, and process the friction of transition—is never a wasted effort. Whether or not this moment ultimately qualifies as a great transformation, the anomalies described in earlier sections are real. People are experiencing them. They will look for ways to address them. The question is whether those ways emerge from lived experience in specific communities or are imposed from a distance by entities optimizing for metrics that may not capture what matters.

Naming the Search

If there is a pattern connecting the clinical interventions that work, the cultural experiments that resonate, and the historical examples of successful integration, it is this: restoring balance between the constructs we build, the material reality we inhabit, and the biological organism that mediates between them.

The Latin phrase Vita Plena Sensu—life full of sense—captures both dimensions of this balance. Sensus in Latin means both sensation and meaning. In German, Sinn carries both simultaneously. The phrase suggests that sensory grounding and meaningful experience are not separate pursuits but aspects of the same thing. A life rich in direct sensory experience tends also to feel meaningful. A life dominated by abstraction, however productive by external metrics, tends to feel hollow.

The phrase borrows its structure from Vita Activa, Hannah Arendt's concept formulated during an earlier period of reckoning with the consequences of modernity. The echo is deliberate. Arendt diagnosed the modern condition as one of thoughtlessness—not stupidity, but the inability to think clearly amidst the noise of mass society. The current condition might be described as one of senselessness—not irrationality, but the progressive detachment of daily life from sensory coherence.

In German, we might call the direction these corrections point toward Präsenzkultur—a culture organized around presence. Not as ideology or lifestyle brand, but as a structural observation: the interventions that work, across clinical, cultural, and architectural domains, share a common mechanism. They restore the conditions under which a human organism can be present. They complete the optimization by including the variable that was left out.

Whether this constitutes an emerging paradigm in Kuhn's sense—a fundamentally new framework that reorganizes perception—or simply a correction within the existing one is a question this thesis cannot definitively answer. The patterns are visible. Their full significance will only be legible in retrospect, as has always been the case with transitions of this kind. What can be said is that the search is underway, that it is not coordinated by any single institution or movement, and that its direction is consistent enough across independent domains to be worth documenting.
